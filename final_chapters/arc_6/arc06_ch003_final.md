# Chapter 3 — The Little Girl’s Cell

“So how did your talk with the master go?”

“About the same as always. Except that he got on my nerves worse than usual.” Subaru spread his hands. “You can fill in the rest.”

“Yes. I think I understand you well enough.”

They had left the parlor behind, and the task of leading them to the mansion’s east wing had fallen to Frederica. The scary-faced woman, with her long, beautiful blonde hair and her maid’s uniform worn as neatly as a vow, had at last exchanged the proper courtesies with Anastasia and Julius, as befitted one of the mansion’s honest servants—and then she turned to Subaru, who was trudging along at the back of the group looking thoroughly deflated.

“Was Garf any use to you on the road and in the city?” There was a crease of worry under the question. “To think he let himself be hurt badly enough that he couldn’t even come home, after I gave him such careful instructions… I do worry that he’s been a burden to Emilia-sama and the others.”

“Oh, no, don’t worry about that at all,” Emilia said quickly. “Garfiel worked so, so hard for us. He’s resting right now, together with Otto-kun, without causing anyone any trouble… well—I can’t be completely sure about the ‘no trouble’ part. I really hope that isn’t what’s happening. But we did tell them both to rest, at least.”

Frederica inclined her head. “My apologies—for letting my foolish little brother become such a nuisance to you.”

Emilia let the reassurance trail off, since she half-doubted it herself, while Frederica went on quietly apologizing.

In any case, Garfiel’s conduct was a worry to Petra, to Ram, to Roswaal, and to Frederica—yet something in the boy had shifted during the affair at Priestella. He had grown up, there was no other word for it. To begin with, his strength was already remarkable for a boy of fifteen. Even measured against how savage the fights with the Bowel Hunter and the Eight Arms had been, he was still a long way from calling himself the strongest man alive; and yet he undeniably commanded a power that stood among the finest not merely in the Emilia Camp but across the whole continent.

His one great weakness was, perhaps, still that unripe heart of his. Once he mastered that frailty, he would climb another rung entirely. But there was time yet, and it was not a thing to be rushed. To grow stronger steadily and surely—to set his foot firmly on each stair before reaching for the next—was the proper way for a boy of fifteen to come into his own. And there was, besides—

“There’s another reason he didn’t come straight home this time,” Subaru said.

“Is it Mimi?” Emilia asked.

“That’s part of it, sure, but.”

She had cut into his thoughts as if she’d overheard them, and Subaru gave a wry little smile. Even Emilia—whose grasp of romance between a man and a woman was supposedly nonexistent—seemed, it turned out, to have a certain appetite for other people’s love affairs. Garfiel and Mimi’s, in particular, had her thoroughly intrigued.

And Garfiel, of course, ought to feel something for the girl who had thrown her own life between him and death, a girl who had never once been shy about announcing her feelings for him, before the battle or after it. Plainly put, there was no man alive who wouldn’t be shaken to have such open affection thrust upon him by a girl he didn’t dislike. Subaru himself was no exception; having been through it once, he was in no position to say a word about the phenomenon. Garfiel’s own fondness for Ram was clear enough, so how the boy would answer Mimi remained to be seen—but that was a different matter from the one he had left behind in Priestella.

“…Subaru-sama?” Frederica said. “Was there something you wished to ask me?”

“No, nothing in particular. It really isn’t my place to talk about this one.”

He had been studying her profile with a weighty look, and she asked, gently, what he meant by it.

Out of regard for Garfiel’s true feelings, Subaru sidestepped the question. Back in Priestella there was a family the boy had fretted over—people of golden hair and green eyes, the very coloring that would make anyone think at once of Frederica and Garfiel, brother and sister. What Garfiel was to that family was much what he was to Frederica herself. And surely the news of that bond was something he would want to carry home to his own kin, to Frederica and to Ryuzu, in his own words and no one else’s.

“So I’ll keep my mouth shut. Natsuki Subaru is going to let this one lie—in an *ultra-cool* way.”

“Oh, that reminds me,” Emilia said brightly, “speaking of Garfiel—back in Priestella there were some children who were so, so fond of him, and they were—”

“Emilia-tan, Emilia-tan, you’re going to trample my whole monologue!”

His careful discretion was about to be flattened, so Subaru quite literally clapped a hand over Emilia’s mouth to stop the words. The synergy between the two of them, left unchecked, would be a fearsome thing; he made a private note to sit her down later and explain the shape of the situation in full. Then he waved his free hand at Frederica, whose expression was growing more and more mystified, and put on a strained smile.

Frederica’s patience had worn to an edge. “Forgive me for interrupting while the two of you are working yourselves into such a cozy little glow—but have you any notion of where it is we’re going?”

“Oh—that place. The Confinement Room. The one we were talking about before is down there.”

“Hmm. That one,” Julius murmured. “I do wonder whether the conversation will go the way we’d like.”

“It’s never been tried before, so—no idea. Either way, I figure it’s worth asking, just to be sure.”

Julius answered Subaru’s account with a thoughtful silence, following at his back with a complicated look on his face. And in truth, Subaru was well aware of the many problems his proposal carried with it. But if it went well—if it went well, the danger of the road ahead would be cut down by no small margin.

“And that girl is rather fond of Subaru,” Emilia offered, “so it should be all right, shouldn’t it?”

“She’s fonder of Garfiel than of me. Maybe it’s the cat thing that does it for her… hm. We’re here.”

He was still returning Emilia’s rosy optimism with a strained smile when Frederica came to a halt. Subaru and Emilia stopped as well, for they had stepped into a part of the mansion whose whole look and air was distinctly foreign to the rest of it. The floor plan matched the old Roswaal mansion closely enough. It was the basement of the eastern wing, and that alone, that stood so starkly apart.

The central building held the main wing, where everything needed for daily life was gathered close together. The west wing served all-purpose ends—the maids’ quarters, the guest rooms, and the like. And the east wing stood as a storehouse and a vault for the history and the legacy of the ancient Mathers bloodline, its books and its heirlooms. All of it save one place: the east wing’s basement. For that cold room of stone beneath the noble’s house, there had been a single clear purpose in the building of it.

“Not the most pleasant air hangin’ about down here, is it,” Anastasia remarked.

She sniffed once, her curt little comment underscoring how the atmosphere had changed. There was no arguing with her reading of it; it was, plainly, a judgment the whole party shared. The air that hung over this level of the mansion could be called nothing but unpleasant.

“Different from Miasma, seems like.” She rolled her shoulders. “But my body sure isn’t happy about it either way.”

“The structure of the building, and the purpose this place was raised for to begin with…” Julius said slowly. “It may be wholly unrelated—but I believe this place was ultimately built up around the presence that dwells within it.”

Anastasia and Julius traded these words while looking down the long, unbroken flight of stairs that sank into the darkness beneath the mansion. The Confinement Room had already been named for them; it seemed the two of them had guessed rightly enough at the kind of place that waited below. Or had come as near to the truth as made no difference.

“Well then. I shall lead you down,” Frederica said. “Please mind your footing, so that you don’t slip.”

With that she started down the stairs, and Subaru and the rest followed her tall back toward the basement. Julius’s face said he had braced himself for whatever lay ahead. Their footsteps on the stone steps rang out with startling clarity through the enclosed space. A cold draft came climbing up from below, stirring Subaru’s bangs, and for reasons he couldn’t name it set him on edge.

“I am going to open it now,” Frederica said.

At the bottom of the stairs their way was barred by an iron door, and the door was fitted with a great many heavy locks. Frederica worked them loose one by one. The key turned with a sound, the bolts gave, and the door swung inward on a long creak. Beyond the gate a path of stone ran on, and at the far end of it, sunk deep into the back of the place, stood yet another door.

“The one you’re looking for is beyond this,” Frederica said.

She stepped aside and bowed, clearing the way for them to pass. They answered with small nods of the chin and went on toward the door, four of them in a line, Subaru at the head.

—The door at the very deepest part of the basement had no lock at all.

He laid his hand on the knob. One push, and they would surely come face to face with whatever was inside. His fingers on the door, Subaru held his breath and glanced back over his shoulder.

Nobody said a word.

Emilia, Julius, and Anastasia all watched what he was doing. The three of them gave him a nod, and Subaru drew a deep breath and put his strength into the hand that gripped the knob. With a sound, the door came open—

“Roaar—roaaar! I’m gonnaaa eaaat yooou up!”

“Kyaaa—help me, no, nooo!”

“Heh, heh, heh. It’s no good crying for help. Nobody’s coming to save you.”

As the door opened, a sweet little voice reached them, and light came spilling into the passage. On the far side of the doorway a single girl sat in the room, ringed all about by a crowd of dolls. Her back was to them, and she went on making the dolls in her hands dance and speak. She kept slipping into these funny put-on voices—though it was anyone’s guess what she was actually imitating. Whatever it was, she was thoroughly absorbed in it.

“No—he’s coming. Because he promised he’d come for me if I was ever in trouble, after all… hm?”

Clutching a little girl-doll, the child suddenly rose to her feet, as though something had caught her attention. She looked timidly over her shoulder—and found Subaru and the others standing stock-still and dumbfounded in the entrance, at which her large, round eyes went wide. Her light-brown hair was done up in braids, her features simple and sweet.

“Yo. How’s it going?”

Subaru lifted a hand and greeted her as if nothing at all had happened.

“O-onii-san, you *idiot*! At least knock before you barge i-in!”

And, as anyone might have predicted, he got a shrill scolding for his little prank.

“So, everybody—this is the mansion’s one prisoner. Meili here is going to give us the lowdown on Witchbeasts.”

“I’ve been humiliated… onii-san hurt my feelings. Sniffle, sniffle.”

Subaru made his introduction of the sulking girl, who had folded herself into a corner of the room amid a heap of stuffed animals—Meili. Julius shook his head and pressed his fingers to the bridge of his nose. Keeping the girl at the edge of his sight, he spoke as if to reproach Subaru for letting himself get carried away.

“It was careless of me not to have gathered more on this beforehand. But the meanness of your character is on full display. It really is inexcusable, the way you seize on every chance for a prank.”

“I don’t think I’m messing around all that much, though… all right, fine, I’ll grant it’s in poor taste. But you know, she really was a dangerous person, before.”

Julius looked as if he took Subaru’s excuses with more than a grain of salt. Paying no mind to the suspicions of the men, Emilia and Anastasia went and crouched together before Meili.

“I’m sorry, Meili,” Emilia said. “We’ll give Subaru a good scolding for you later, so please don’t be upset.”

“My, what a darling little thing.” Anastasia tilted her head. “Beatrice-chan, Petra-chan—wouldn’t you say this tells you an awful lot about Natsuki-kun’s tastes?”

“That’s a monstrous thing to say about a guy! Could you please pick something a little more flattering?! I set out to gather *people*, not specifically little girls!”

His nickname, Lolimancer, had suddenly acquired a whiff of legitimacy, and he dearly wished she wouldn’t say such frightening things. That aside, Meili had paid no more heed to Emilia’s soothing than to anything else; she was sunk in genuine indignation. In that respect she was still very much a child, prone to a child’s tender, lopsided feelings.

“Meili,” Subaru said.

“Can’t hear you.”

“Meili, come on.”

“Don’t care.”

So it had come to this. She was a handful, having slid into the mood where she’d refuse to hear a word from anyone. Subaru resolved, therefore, to play his last card as quickly as he could. He reached around behind his back and produced, for Meili’s benefit, the ace up his sleeve.

“Look here, Meili. A present—take it. It’s a brand-new stuffed animal. Darepanda.”

“—! Wow! It’s so cu-ute!”

What Subaru held out to her was a stuffed toy he had patched together during the long dragon-carriage ride home from Priestella. Over this past year of honing his skills in the domestic arts, Natsuki Subaru’s needlework had grown twice as good as it once was. He could turn out a stuffed animal now, and if the whim took him, he might even manage a dress.

At any rate, this new toy was a bold invention that married the theme of a panda to a certain languid, worn-out-by-the-heat sensibility. He had the faint feeling he’d seen something rather like it somewhere before—but since that somewhere lay beyond this world, it was no matter, and he let it go. And so he presented his latest creation, with all its little touches, and Meili received it with her eyes shining.

“Cute! It’s a brand-new animal! Now, what should I name it… okay, I’ve got it! I’ll call this one Big Panda!”

“I see you named it exactly what it is.”

Her naming sense aside, she had an unerring instinct for the essence of a thing. Hugging the stuffed panda close and lovingly, she looked up at Subaru and the others with a face that showed her spirits had been thoroughly rescued.

“By the way—welcome home, onii-san, onee-san. Looks like you were gone a good long while. You know, Petra-chan missed you.”

“We were away a whole month, after all. And we’ll be gone a while again soon, so I’d honestly rather not think about how furious that’s going to make Petra, but…”

“Petra-chan really does love onii-san, you know… hm? New people?”

She was lining her scattered plush toys back onto their shelves when Meili suddenly noticed Julius and Anastasia, and it showed plainly on her face. Julius was taken aback by the shift in her mood, but Anastasia only gave a small, unruffled nod.

“Set against Mimi, the feeling of bein’ played with barely rises past the surface.”

“…Now that you mention it, I agree,” Julius said. “Let us give our thanks to Mimi.”

Master and servant shared this strange moment of accord, and afterward Julius’s eyes moved swiftly about the room. Once he had taken the underground space in at a glance, he said:

“All the same—I had assumed, on hearing the words ‘Confinement Room,’ that it would be a harsh place. Yet against every expectation, it seems a rather peaceful spot to pass one’s time.”

Emilia’s face clouded over. “The person we keep here is a little girl, so it isn’t as though we wanted to make her suffer… but she can’t go outside, either. It’s complicated.”

And just as she said, the Confinement Room—or, to put it bluntly, Meili’s living quarters—had a decidedly gentle feeling for a place of imprisonment. The stone passage did lend it a chill, true enough, but bright wallpaper and carpet dressed the girl’s room, and she was free to do as she pleased, bound by nothing. On the shelves stood countless stuffed toys made by Subaru’s own hands, alongside a wealth of books and playthings.

She was fed, and there was no trouble over baths or the privy. In short, it was a place where a *hikikomori* could live in perfect peace—so comfortable that even Subaru half wished to be shut up in it. And yet—

Julius’s gaze traveled slowly along the walls. “There is a peculiar air about it. Something akin to Miasma, seeping out without end.”

“And it seems to be comin’ from that girl… that’s it, isn’t it,” Anastasia said.

Meili answered the look Anastasia and the others turned on her with a smiling nod. It was an honest smile—yet something poured off the child, something so dreadful and so foul that no amount of sweetness could hide it. That was the chief reason Meili had been shut away here and was not set free.

Subaru scratched at the back of his neck. “I mentioned it back in the dragon carriage—but this girl actually came gunning for Emilia and the rest of us, once. Hmm. So she’s basically a hired killer. That’s about the size of it, right?”

“I feel there is already something amiss even at that stage, but by all means, go on,” Julius put in.

“There’s a whole lot of meaning packed into how you said that… anyway. She’s a professional. And as for what Meili’s little body can do—in short, she can command Witchbeasts. She’s a Witchbeast Master.”

“Yep. Me and the naughty animal-sans get along re-eal well.”

She said it with an “ahem,” puffing herself up with pride, but the substance of it was appalling. Witchbeasts were a scourge upon mankind, never in any age friendly toward humans. There was, it was said, a single exception—an arrangement whereby a Witchbeast would obey the one who had broken its horn—but—

“In Meili’s case, the horn’s got nothing to do with it,” Subaru said. “I’m shaky on the theory, so even if I tried to explain it, it wouldn’t land.”

“The way Mama put it, I play the same part a Witchbeast’s horn does. So-o, the Witchbeasts and I can be friends.”

What it meant to stand in for a Witchbeast’s horn was murky at best. Then again, it seemed no proper study had ever been made of the creatures’ nature. There were surely people who hunted Witchbeasts for a living, of course—but even if such folk held some lore about the beasts’ habits, they would hardly have looked at them through a scholar’s eyes.

“At first, Meili came to attack us with one other person,” Emilia said. “Garfiel defeated that person, and this girl was caught. She’s been sheltered here in the mansion ever since.”

“Why go to such lengths?” Anastasia asked. “Say what you like—if she was your enemy once, you should’ve dealt with her then and there… or, wait. It doesn’t seem this child *can* be dealt with.”

“…There’s no possible way we could bring ourselves to kill her,” Emilia said. “But at the same time, we can’t turn her out, either. Because this girl says that if we let her go—”

“Mama will be furious with me,” Meili finished. “Elsa died, and I failed, so if she finds me she’ll kill me for certain. So the safest thing is to stay right here.”

Meili gave her answer in a calm, level voice, yet a shadow seemed to fall across her face as she spoke. She had lost Elsa, her partner in the work, and failed to carry out the task set for her. Whoever their guardians were, they were most likely something like managers of hired killers—and a failure of that kind would never, ever be forgiven.

To be cast out would mean receiving her punishment; and that was her own affair, a matter with which Subaru and the others had nothing whatever to do—

“It really is a nightmare of a situation,” Subaru said.

“This is hardly the first time Emilia-sama’s thinking and yours have been laid out for us,” Julius said. “It was out of turn for us outsiders to say such foolish things… by the by, who is this ‘Mama’ of hers?”

“We don’t know. Beyond the fact that the mother goes by ‘Mama,’ the woman’s a total blank. By what Meili tells us, she doesn’t even know her own mother’s face. What kind of home is that?”

Still—given the trade of a professional killer, perhaps so bleak a way of living couldn’t be helped. It seemed hopeless to work out who the mother was from anything in Meili’s account, and the party could not shake their unease about what lay ahead.

Subaru’s voice dropped low. “That bastard Roswaal was no use either, once Elsa was dead. No one left to act as a go-between.”

“Did you say something?” Julius asked.

“Just talking to myself.”

The Insignia affair on the very first day—the day Subaru had been called to this world—had been staged in the Royal Capital by Roswaal’s hand. And Elsa, of course, who had tried to reap Emilia’s life, had been the one he’d hired for the deed. From the shape of that commission, the conclusion should have been easy enough to draw.

But the go-between on that line of communication had somehow perished, and it seemed there was now no way to reach the woman. If Roswaal’s word could be trusted at all, then the attack Elsa and Meili had made afterward lay outside his designs—and this was precisely where the “Mother,” or whatever she was, became a thing to be feared.

“Anyway, that’s where we stand with Meili,” Subaru said. “She’s here because she has to be. It isn’t about coddling her—not for now, at least.”

“Even so, Subaru and everyone are being just a little too nice about it,” Meili said.

“Ah, well, as for that—it’s our dreamy good-naturedness taking the wheel!”

Because, when it came down to it, there was something painful in the sight of so small and young a girl locked away in a cold cellar. It wasn’t a thing one could simply resolve to forget; try to look past it, and you’d only end up plagued by bad dreams. He bore her no grudge, and so it suited him well enough to keep things pleasant between them.

“…Wouldn’t a body normally hold a grudge against her, though?” Anastasia said.

“…Should I?”

“I mean, didn’t you say she tried to kill you? So…”

“There’s such a thing as pointless, idle malice—though a crime’s a crime, whatever the age of the one who commits it.”

At Anastasia’s question, Subaru fell to thinking, scratching his cheek. When he stole a glance at Meili beside him, he found her watching him, some feeling he couldn’t read swimming in her eyes. He wasn’t especially bothered by it, and he spoke his mind plainly.

“If somebody orders a person who doesn’t know any better to commit a crime, then it’s the one who gave the order who ought to be blamed. All the more so if it’s a child. But if they’re capable of thinking for themselves and do it anyway, then I figure it makes no difference. ‘An eye for an eye’—I think the Code of Hammurabi’s got nothing going for it but the downside.”

“Fine words,” Anastasia said. “So—do you suppose the people this girl has murdered would agree with you?”

“I doubt it. They’re free to want their revenge on Meili. As for me—if I’d been the one killed, I don’t imagine even I would talk about forgiveness the way I just did.”

In the end, Subaru’s opinions and his sympathies swung on first impressions. Tell him he wasn’t right in the head, or that his feelings ruled him, and—so be it. In fact, it was probably true.

“When I was a kid, my parents and the other grown-ups took responsibility for me,” he said. “So I figured—if there’s a kid near me who does something they can’t answer for, why not answer for it in their place? That’s all it is.”

“…A valuable opinion. My thanks,” Anastasia said.

She cut him off there, her interest having drained away somewhere along the line. He’d expected as much, honestly, and it did his feelings no injury. In an organization where clean, considered judgment counted for nothing, any crime would be judged a crime, whatever the age of the offender. Here a grace period was granted, only because this camp of theirs was a lenient one. Subaru did not dislike that air, that way of doing things.

“I…” Julius began.

“—Hm?”

“I believe there is sense in what Subaru says.”

“…Thanks.”

He had not been braced for wholehearted agreement, and words like those came to him as something close to salvation. Then he turned to Meili—forgotten, though she’d been the very subject of the talk—and met her eyes with his own.

“There’s something I need your help with. Would you come along with me?”

“…Oka-ay. I’ll go with onii-san.”

Meili nodded quietly at his request. And neither Subaru nor anyone else remarked on how they had seemed to catch the faint glint of tears in the girl who had hidden her face against the panda in her arms.

“I’ve only ever been to the Augria Sand Dunes twice.”

Subaru and the others had finished laying it all out, and Meili spoke while toying with one of her braids. She shut her eyes, as though looking back over her memories, before she went on.

“I went to top up the Witchbeasts under my control. There are lots of them out there, so I got a good deal done, but…”

“But?” Subaru prompted.

“Onii-san—are you really going out there? Everyone but me will probably die, you know…?”

To the eyes of this girl unburdened by any morals, their plan looked like plain recklessness. And the Witchbeast Master’s judgment carried no small weight as to just how far over their heads they were reaching.

Subaru huffed out a breath. “I’d love to lean on the wisdom of somebody with real experience. But ‘go out there and you’ll die’ is a line that’s already been thrown at us more times than I can count, so it’s a little late for that. Besides—if it’s just a matter of a maze in the desert, we’ve got the means to break out of it.”

“That’d be me, yes,” Anastasia said, waving a hand to underscore her part as their guide.

Even so, in this campaign to cross the desert and reach the Watchtower, simply not losing their way was worth all of thirty points—a score that guaranteed failure. Or rather, until they could find an answer worth a perfect hundred, they would have their work cut out for them merely getting out alive.

There were three problems: the Desert Labyrinth, the Witchbeast Den, and the Sage’s Eye. The Labyrinth would sort itself out with Anastasia standing in for Echidna; what wanted a countermeasure was the Den. That was why he’d hoped to draw more out of Meili—but.

“How should I put it… you don’t know any way to steer clear of the Witchbeasts? Or, the other way round—some way to lure them off away from us?”

“Onii-san, if you go running off on your own, a wh-ole lot of them will chase after you, for sure.”

“I’ve tried that a few times already, and every one of them was miserable.”

Once, a year ago, at the mansion; and once more, a little after, out on the plain when the Whale came. He rather felt it was about time to retire the tactic. He wasn’t unwilling to fall back on it if there were truly no other course—but if it could be helped, Subaru would sooner not end up the only man left standing in the middle of the desert.

“If onii-san doesn’t want to do it himself,” Meili said, “then maybe you could get lots of decoys ready. Every Witchbeast will leap at something living before it touches ordinary food—so, something along those lines.”

“That’s absolutely out! I’m against it—I am completely against it!” Emilia cried.

“You don’t have to object quite so frantically. Nobody’s going to be chosen for it, so it’s fine.”

That said, no useful notions seemed to be surfacing. Or, at the least, where the Witchbeasts’ nature was concerned, if Meili—who handled them better than anyone—couldn’t produce a workable plan, then they hadn’t much to go on.

“Right—guess we’re left with cutting down the Witchbeasts one by one as they come at us. I’ll be counting on Emilia-tan and Julius for a lot of that.”

“I wonder at your estimate of your own worth, seeing as you left yourself off that list,” Julius said. “But—do you truly think it can be done?” And he put the question of its feasibility to Meili. Hearing him, she looked from Emilia to Julius and back, and said:

“Probably not. Can onee-san keep using magic for about a week without any rest?”

“You want us fighting some last-ditch battle in a trench!?” Subaru said.

“I-I’ll try…?” Emilia offered.

“You don’t have to! This is just how an unreasonable conversation runs away with itself! Emilia-tan’s hair and skin will dry right out—so let’s not! There, rejected!”

It seemed the plan to bull their way through by force already carried some outlandish demands. How much of that picture Meili had actually witnessed was anyone’s guess, but if there was no childish exaggeration in what she said, then the brute-force road was one they could never walk.

“How about smearing Witchbeast blood over the dragon carriage, and fooling the other Witchbeasts’ noses?” Emilia offered.

“No good—the ground dragons won’t be fooled by it, and there’s every chance the Witchbeasts wouldn’t hunt in a pack anyway. Something like hiring guards, then… what if we brought on a whole army for this, the way we did with the White Whale?”

“That, too, will not do,” Julius said. “It has been tried before, by others who challenged the Pleiades Watchtower. Not an army, quite, but a large company all the same. You may guess the result for yourself. What of breaking the horn of one powerful Witchbeast and using it to hold off the rest?”

“If it’s not something as overwhelming as the Whale, we’ll just get swamped by sheer numbers. Sure would be nice if slipping past them were as easy as—I don’t know, spraying on some bug repellent.”

But Subaru’s own scent—the Witch’s Lingering Scent—was less repellent than lure; it was a spray that drew the pests straight to him, which made him worse than useless. It was Roswaal who saw to the endless upkeep of the barrier that walled Arlam Village off from the Witchbeasts’ territory. Something like raising a barrier around people—one that traveled along with them—did not, it seemed, lie within reach.

“Maybe we get Roswaal to carry us and strike from the sky,” Subaru said.

“Were we able to slip past the Sage’s Eye, that might have been an option,” Julius replied.

“Ah—damn, right. We’ve still got the Sage to reckon with too.”

His unrealistic idea was struck down by a realistic one. Even supposing they could give the Witchbeasts and the desert the slip, there remained the Sage to worry over. How was it that no one, ever, had managed to steal past this person? It was, in all honesty, the single piece of the puzzle they hadn’t the faintest idea how to solve.

“If we could just narrow it down to one problem…” Subaru said.

“—Geez. I guess I’ve got no choice, then,” Meili said.

“Oh?”

They had all been sitting in a ring on the floor of the room, nursing headaches as they cast about for a way out of the tangle, when Meili at last spoke up. She got to her feet and gave her small head a little shake at them.

“I can come with you. If I’m there, the Witchbeasts won’t care what any of you do. You’ll be free to keep clear of them, or make pets of them, or set them killing each other—or have them eat the Sage person right up.”

“We’re not doing that last part! But—well…”

His eyes went wide at the frankly extreme suggestion, yet the offer itself shocked him more. Not only was Meili willing to cooperate—she was willing to go out into the world herself, the very thing he’d been sure she would never propose.

“Aren’t you scared to go outside? Are you sure about this?”

“It’s not like Mama’s going to find me the moment I set foot out of the mansion, you know? I’m scared of being found, sure. But I can’t stay shut up in here my whole life.”

It caught him off guard, that Meili should be so aware that sooner or later she would have to leave. And yet perhaps it was only natural. He’d looked in on her whenever the chance came, taking his turn with the others—but even so, the hours the girl spent shut alone in that room outnumbered them many times over. She’d had time enough, surely, for the silence to grow frightening. To be confined alone in a room was at once a rest and a terror; and even if such a life lulled a person into dullness, there would come a day when it would seize the heart and wring it without warning.

“Subaru…” Emilia murmured.

And while Subaru was feeling an unaccustomed sympathy for the state of Meili’s heart, Emilia gave his sleeve a gentle tug. He knew what she was thinking, and he thought the same.

“This isn’t a nice little stroll outdoors,” he said. “We’ve got a guide, we’ll be crossing a desert, and even with you along, we’ll still have to fight our way through the Witchbeast Den. Worse than that, some frightening Sage might be watching the whole way.”

“Isn’t it lovely, taking a walk after so long?” Meili said.

With those game words she answered him. How much of it was bluster he couldn’t tell, and neither could he tell how much was the plain truth, but—

“She’s our trump card against the Witchbeasts, isn’t she?” Emilia said.

“Ah—yeah, that’s right. We’ll be counting on you, Meili.”

“I’ll do my very best, even if you don’t,” Meili said.

Emilia and Subaru traded a look, and then they both nodded to her. The girl to whom their words were addressed answered as if she meant to push herself past the very limit of her strength, and hugged the panda tight. And so this became one step forward toward the journey’s end—at which Julius folded his arms, and a few words spilled out of him, his face full of admiration for what he had just watched unfold.

“I truly wish I did not have to say it—but talking little girls around really is one of your gifts. Though I can’t say it’s a talent one would care to be renowned for.”

“It’s because all of you keep steadily branding me a little-girl-handler! And I’ll have you know Meili isn’t even as small as a little girl! She is *not* a little girl!”

Subaru jabbed a finger toward the room’s entrance in his exasperation at Julius’s needling. And at that very moment the door swung open, and someone appeared on the other side of it.

“—I was wondering what all the noise was, only to find it’s simply Subaru kicking up a fuss again, I suppose.”

Her private word with Roswaal concluded, yet another little girl had come to join them. There was a fresh commotion after that—but it was a small thing, not worth the telling, and so it will be left where it lies.
