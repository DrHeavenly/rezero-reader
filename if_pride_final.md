# Pride IF

Heat — a violent heat low in his stomach — took up the whole of his mind.

"Ghhgg — hot!"

His cheek lay against the hard floor. He understood, dimly, that he had fallen and now sprawled face-down; but his limbs would not lift him, and lay where they had dropped, as though the body were no longer his, as though it had been stolen out from under him. And still the fire went on burning him, patient and thorough, so that there could be nothing false in any of it.

*Hot. Hot, hot, hothothothothot.*

He opened his mouth, and what came out in place of a scream was a slow welling of blood. Half drowning in it, he was given over to the very summit of pain.

*What in the world had he done?*

He begged — that small, whining, cowardly plea a man makes to be let out of his suffering — and it went round and round inside his head. What had he done? He had never pretended his was a life worth open praise. But then, that was true of more than him alone. No one lives so clean a life that he can boast of it to the whole of mankind; men feel guilt, men feel remorse, men look the other way, men strike their small bargains. So why was it he, and he alone, who had to bear this? Why should the fate that every other soul was allowed to ignore come and fasten itself upon him only?

"Ah — damn…"

The whisper spilled out between the streams of blood, carrying his remorse, and his contempt for his own helplessness, and his hatred of fate —

*— and, under all of it, his exasperation with himself.*

"—"

And yet. Even with all this ruin in him, all this agony, all this dying — even if the fire burned him down to nothing, even if the pain broke him, even if it took his life — the girl fixed in his mind would go on smiling, and go on meeting the same wretched death, again and again without end. And so —

"I—"

Once more he gave the words: his resolve, his resolution, his rue, his regret — reaching, still, for a future that no number of attempts, no number of desperate scrambles, no number of wishes would ever be enough to bring within his grasp.

The pain and the heat and the whole of the world drew away from him, and, like the whipped dog he was, he lifted his head and howled.

"—No matter what—"

The blade came down, indifferent, upon the last dying ember of his life. He never even saw it. He had already reached his decision.

*— I will save you.*

And in the instant he renewed that wish, Natsuki Subaru lost his life.

***

The scene that met him was a familiar one, and the sight of it left him strangely relieved, and strangely tired.

"So. Made it back in one piece, and nothing left to be afraid of — I suppose."

He said it to himself, watching the traffic of the Royal Capital while he scratched a row of tally marks — four uprights and a slash — into the dirt with a stick. He kept at it until the count satisfied him, then scuffed the marks away with his foot and let out a breath.

Natsuki Subaru hailed from the third planet out from the sun, namely Earth; his rank in life, a perfectly unremarkable third-year of high school who never went to class. Anyone taking in the casual getup — the tracksuit, the sneakers, the plastic convenience-store bag swinging from one hand — would not have doubted it for a moment. Provided, of course, that the observer came from some corner of the world where "tracksuits" and "sneakers" and "convenience stores" meant anything at all. Which brought him to the point he was really trying to make.

"…This whole 'spirited away to another world' business is a lot rougher than I figured it'd be."

A carriage drawn by a lizard the size of a horse went past in a spray of dust. Such a thing had of course been impossible in the world he came from. One might have argued it was only a horse dressed up in elaborate costume, some mascot out of a theme park — but Subaru would not have agreed, because the lizards were not one but many, teeming along every street of the city, and the people who drove them were themselves got up in a great deal of costume: beast-folk, demihumans, men and women wearing the shapes of animals. Twenty or thirty of them stood within his field of view alone. There were ordinary-looking people too, of his own make, plenty of them; but the sheer riot of the others swallowed them up and pushed them clean out of his notice.

He could not stand here forever, though, running from what was in front of him. The beastmen and the lizards were no part of any film set or stage production. This scene was the plain, ordinary truth of the place, and the thing out of place in it was not them — it was Subaru. After ten-odd days of living inside that fact, he had come to understand it to a painful degree.

"…Time to move."

Done with the useless brooding, he slapped the dust off the seat of his pants and got to his feet. Keeping the stick in his right hand, he wandered down off the main road — not into the crowd where he could vanish, but the other way, into the back lanes. The backstreet, unlike the thoroughfare, was deserted, crammed between tall buildings, silent: a single narrow lane sealed off from the world outside. Which was to say, a place where something could happen and the screams would never reach the main road. So that when an obvious outsider like him strayed into it, the men who preyed on such outsiders were bound, sooner or later, to appear.

"Hey. Buddy. Come play with us a minute."

He turned toward the voice and found three shapes filling the mouth of the alley: a big one, a middling one, and a little one, a tidy general-issue set of them. He could feel them sizing him up — what exactly they thought they were measuring, he had no idea.

"—"

Subaru tipped his head and looked past them, back the way he'd come. The alley ended in a dead wall behind him; the only way out was to have the three of them let him by. And as to whether the three had the slightest intention of letting him back onto the main street —

"The hell you spacing out for?"

"He don't get what's going on yet. Let's help him find out."

His slackness set them grinning, obscenely, the ideas for what to do with him falling into place behind their eyes. From where they stood he was a rookie, plain as day; no one could blame a man for licking his lips at easy pickings like that.

And they were wrong. Utterly, completely wrong. It was true that Subaru had no real experience of fighting, no hidden past in which he'd studied some martial art; rookie was the one part of their reckoning that held. But where fighting these three particular men was concerned — Subaru was already a veteran.

"—Huh?"

At the big one, the inattentive giant — Tom, of the Tom, Dick, and Larry he'd privately named them — Subaru's arm shot out. The stick was still in his grip, blunt as it was, and it drove into the soft of the man's throat and, with only a little resistance, sank into his neck.

"Wha—"

Tom's eyes flew wide at the suddenness of it; Dick and Larry went rigid. Tom was finished. While the other two stood frozen, Subaru's free hand darted for the middle one — Dick — caught him by the hair and the ear, and swung. He allowed no resistance. Riding the momentum, he smashed Dick's skull into the wall. Something hard crunched and split, and Dick left a long smear of blood down the stone as he folded to the ground. In the same motion Subaru drove his knee into the impaled Tom, whose eyes bulged as he pitched over — badly, forward, so that the stick only buried itself the deeper.

Two of them down, then. One left.

"Eep."

The little one, Larry, went white watching the pair of them fall in an instant. Had he cut and run for the street then and there, it would have come down to his legs against Subaru's, and he might have lived. Instead he looked at his fallen friends and dithered over whether to bolt — not knowing it was already far too late for them — and so he squandered the single second he had.

*Fool. Idiot. He'd have his reward for a decision that stupid.*

"Ghau — ghhg—"

Subaru closed both hands around the thin neck and bore Larry back against the wall, squeezing, lifting, until the man hung level with his own eyes. Larry's face swelled, his mouth working open and shut after air that would not come; but the windpipe was crushed shut, and there would be no saving him.

"So — do you get it yet? How many times, all told, I've run into you three?"

"Khhg… hgh…"

"Eighty-eight. Aren't you lucky, the way your tally only ever goes up. That was a joke. You're supposed to laugh."

He said it glaring into the face below him, running now with spit and tears and a rising darkness of blood. Larry, naturally, had no room left to answer — no room, in fact, for anything; the strength went out of him all at once, and Subaru let the limp body drop to the ground.

He looked the three of them over where they lay, and then, to be sure, began stamping on their necks. Once he felt something give beneath his heel, he could rest easy. Tom's neck was so thick it took five good stamps to manage — Subaru had simply never gotten the knack of that one, though every so often his luck was in and he got them all with a single blow apiece.

"Strangling was a mistake. …Feels foul, too. Won't do it that way again."

The moment of reflection past, he stripped a pair of knives off Dick, then dragged the bodies to the blind end of the alley, left them heaped there, and walked back out of the lane as though nothing at all had happened. Coming into the alley and meeting Tom, Dick, and Larry had taken hardly any time; dealing with them had taken a little more, but in the end less than a minute all told. And then Subaru was hurrying down the main road again, in pursuit of where he needed to be.

"—"

He scanned the street and, with a hand pressed to his chest, let his relief out. In time. This was a great artery of the city called Shopper's Lane, thronged and clamorous like all the rest, charged with the duty of showing off the Capital's health and prosperity. You could stand on it doing nothing and your ears alone would be filled to overflowing with the din. But into that clamor, now, came a change.

"—Wait! Ah — please, wait!"

A voice like a silver bell cut clean through the noise of the market. For all the desperation crowded into it, it could not hide the kindness of the one it belonged to; and the one it was calling to was a small figure that came bursting out of the crowd.

"Heh, heh, hee."

The blonde girl laughed as she threaded through the press of bodies, grinning like a cat. Something sparkled in her fist, and everything in her bearing announced that the job was done. Down the length of the street, aimed at that girl, came a shimmering blue light — a spear of ice.

"—!"

Caught off guard, the girl sprang and twisted aside from the icicle. The spell had been loosed in a street packed with people; the suddenness of it threw the crowd into confusion, and in an instant they cleared a path, arms raised to show they wanted no part of the fight. It was the natural response. To say that being swept up in a brawl was an everyday affair in the Capital would be putting it strongly — and yet perhaps not so far from the truth. In any case, sprinting down the path the crowd had opened came the very person Subaru had been searching for.

"—"

The instant his eyes fell on her, the whole world seemed to freeze. The wind, the voices, time itself dropped out of his awareness, everything in him drawn to a single point and that point her. Her long silver hair streamed out behind her; her amethyst eyes burned with a fierce will. Her slender pale arms and legs were dressed in white so otherworldly the garment might have been sewn for a fairy — and in this world stopped dead of time, she alone was granted motion, and she swept past him without a glance.

Her aim was the blonde girl who had just run by — Felt, who had stolen something from her and was now racing across the Capital to be rid of it. Which was precisely the road that led her toward a doom she could hardly escape.

But Subaru would not let it come to that. He would never let a death like that close its jaws on her.

"I will save you. No matter what."

Watching her draw away from him, he swore the vow for the eighty-eighth time. For a man who had broken it so many times over, there was no telling how much the words were worth. No telling. But if he kept on without surrender, if he kept fighting, if he kept on wishing to save her —

"Wait for me. —Satella."

***

Return by Death: the power to turn time back to the moment of one's dying. Eighty-eight times, now, Subaru had used it to redo the world.

He was closing in on a hundred attempts, and every one of them had been spent on one thing — dragging the silver-haired girl, Satella, back from the dead end of her fate.

"I've run the trial and error on this, believe me… but it isn't going to go the way it does with Tom, Dick, and Larry."

That was the conclusion he had reached about the single greatest enemy standing in this fate of his: Elsa Granhiert. It was Elsa — this woman of shadows — who had put Felt up to stealing Satella's insignia; Elsa who had brought Satella to her death more times than he could count. Across these past eighty-seven attempts Subaru had tried in every way he knew to remove her and make Satella safe, and every time he had come apart against her impossible skill at killing, his belly laid open on better than fifty of those deaths. A sadist who took her pleasure in spilling people open was not an opponent he could beat in a straight fight. Men like Tom, Dick, and Larry he could read, could find the pattern in and plan a certain victory around; but against something as strong as Elsa it was only reasonable that his head should leave his shoulders the moment he tried anything at all. And though it was not, in fact, his head — he had been opened from gut to spine, instantly, more times than he cared to remember. He could not fight her and win. Neither could Felt, nor Rom, nor Satella.

Having come to that conclusion, the course Subaru chose was —

"Wonderful! Beautiful! Though I'd be happier still if you'd let me enjoy myself a little longer!"

"GhhhaaaaAAH—!"

Each time the blades combed the air, blood leapt after them. More bodies lay strewn across the ground than a man could count on his fingers, while Elsa, drenched in it, rose past mere ecstasy and seemed to be sipping her rapture in slow, steady draughts. Ghastly as the scene was, her sensuality only deepened with every fresh layer of blood she bathed in. There are old tales of women who bathed in the blood of virgins to keep their youth, and were called vampires for it; and just now Elsa looked the very image of one.

"…Another failure, then."

Subaru sighed, narrowing his eyes at the grisly scene from a rooftop. He had picked a ward of the slums that was especially run-down and abandoned; the stage for the fight was a space one might loosely call a square, in which the slaughter mounted all in one direction. Those crossing blades with Elsa, and being cut down by them without pause, were the Capital's own guardsmen. A tip from an upstanding citizen had put them onto the dangerous Elsa Granhiert; they had gone to make the arrest, and now their lives lay scattered and butchered across the stones — and since he was that upstanding citizen, the sight put a real ache in Subaru's chest.

"Didn't reckon on the gap between ordinary guards and Elsa being this wide…"

All he had done was set lambs before her to be gladly slaughtered. He had assumed that men charged with guarding the Capital would first arm themselves with some magic or other and then meet Elsa on something like even ground. His hopes, it seemed, had been pitched far too high. So — yes. Even measured against the standards of this world, Elsa was overwhelming. His hatred of her banked higher. And however furious he grew, Subaru would never be strong enough to stop this wicked woman; nor did he believe, for a single instant, that the guards would land some lucky blow and fell her themselves.

"No sense watching any longer."

He was sorry for the guards being sacrificed, but here was where he would put an end to the experiment. He meant to hold nothing back this time; and if in the end he failed and lost his life, then all who had died here would rise again in the reset world. For now he would swallow his tears and let the sacrifices lie. Next time he would ask nothing whatever of them, and they could go back to keeping the Capital's peace with no idea of any of it. Having settled it, he turned to leave the scene — when.

"—Oh, my."

Kukri lifted high, Elsa went still, and licked the blood from her lips. The delirium and bloodlust pouring off her told the story plainly enough: bored, now, with butchering guardsmen, she had found herself new game. And the identity of that luckless quarry was —

"—Come no further."

*A pillar of fire had risen in the square,* was Subaru's first, hallucinating thought.

"—"

He caught his breath, strained his eyes, looked harder — and saw at last that it was no flame but a man standing there. A young man, with hair like fire and eyes as blue as a clear sky. Lean he might have looked, but beneath the white regalia the frame was corded and supple, and at his hip rode a sword larger than any ordinary make. A hundred people out of a hundred would have turned to look as he passed; the beauty of him was such that this handiwork of God would have charmed any eye that fell on it — yet it stirred none of the confusion that beauty beyond either sex can stir, perhaps because of the calm he carried. One glance was all it took for Subaru's soul to tell him: this man was cut from other cloth than the usual riffraff.

"Sir Reinhardt!"

"Everyone, fall back. That woman is the Bowel Hunter." His eyes lowered as he answered the trembling guardsman. "The dead already number far too many. I've no wish to see that count rise."

It sounded as though he grieved for lives that had been butchered like so much vermin, and as though he burned at the murderer, Elsa, who had done it. Even Subaru, watching from a distance, could make out the shape of this young man Reinhardt. His reasoning was just, his ideals were just, the very cast of his mind was just — and so he was angry. He mourned the dead, he loathed the killing, he grieved the failure, and he wrote his remorse over with his conviction. That was the way of this man, this knight.

"Reinhardt van Astrea. Of the Sword Saint's line. Wonderful — excellent!"

"I often feel crushed under the weight of what people expect." He returned her gaze levelly. "And you are the Bowel Hunter?"

"Yes, so I am." Elsa breathed the words out warm and slow. "Oh, goodness — whatever am I to do? I've my work to be getting on with, and here I've gone and come face to face with you."

She looked at him with a fevered ardor; his own face was gravity itself, his eyes full of duty and not a mote of frivolity in them. Two people set against each other from wholly opposite grounds — and yet their hearts arrived at one and the same answer. Each must cut the other down, and so reach what they came for.

"By rights, this is the point at which I would counsel you to surrender," Reinhardt said. "But—"

"With all this slaughter laid out in front of you, and you say that to me? How kind of you. So kind it's cruel — to me, and to them both."

"—No. I am of your mind." He shook his head slowly. "It would be an offense against their deaths to go gently with you. I cannot ask you to surrender."

Elsa gave one of the corpses a cheerful little kick. Reinhardt turned to the guardsman beside him and said, simply, "A sword, if you would."

"Please — take it."

The man held one out, and Reinhardt took it and tested the balance of it in his hands, while Elsa knit her brows, plainly finding something to object to.

"You won't use the one on your hip? And here I'd hoped to feel the bite of the fabled Dragon Sword."

"Unfortunately, this blade decides for itself which opponents it will consent to be drawn against. A troublesome habit — and it seems you haven't earned its favor. I'll meet you with this instead."

"Hmph."

Elsa let out a sigh at him, at the man who had readied not the sword at his waist but the borrowed one. Her displeasure lasted only an instant, though; she shifted at once, and, scenting the battle and the blood to come, licked her lips.

"Elsa Granhiert. The Bowel Hunter."

"Reinhardt van Astrea, of the Sword Saint's line."

They gave each other their names, and both became wind, and hurled themselves forward. The fight was settled in that single exchange. One utterly plain stroke of the sword gave birth to a maelstrom of light, and the shock of it tore that whole quarter of the slums apart. The stroke of the man they called the Sword Saint was the very definition of the word power. Natsuki Subaru watched it with his eyes wide open — a tear on his cheek, his knees trembling under him, and no idea in the world why.

***

It had gone otherwise than Subaru had arranged and foreseen, but it was fair to say the chief aim — Elsa's defeat — had been met. He had not counted on Reinhardt stepping in; still, since it granted his wish, he was more than content. The whole trouble around Satella's insignia would now be safely laid to rest. And so long as Felt and Rom never rejoined the woman who had hired them, it should be no great feat for them to keep the insignia stowed away, and Subaru doubted Satella would fly into a rage and kill the pair of them. The little story that turned upon the Loot House would resolve itself with the barest handful of players — which suited Subaru as well as anyone. And so —

"—Why would you go and help me, of all things?"

"You don't have to trust me. If you'd rather be ringed in by guards and lose every last chance at revenge, then kill me and run — be my guest."

He spat the words from where he leaned against the wall, speaking down at the crouching Elsa. She was painted in blood, her black garment torn open in places to bare the white skin beneath; but that skin was so overlaid with terrible wounds that it left him not the smallest room for embarrassment. And in any case he felt nothing of that sort for Elsa. He had merely judged that she might prove useful, and meant to make what he could of the chance.

"The guards are half mad hunting for you. I've sent them off looking the wrong way, so it'll be a little while before anyone notices us. Your wounds?"

"They hurt. Terribly — terribly, so much I could die of it. Huhu. It's wonderful."

"There's a sentiment I'll never share. It'll be a problem if you die on me — I've a mind to do something with you yet."

Subaru had gone out of his way, into real danger, misdirecting the search so that Elsa might live. Were he cut down here, or swept up in the hunt for her, this stray path on which Satella had survived would crumble to nothing. Though even if it did, it only meant more casts of the dice to bring these events round again.

"Can you open me a road to the southeastern quarter of the slums? If you do, I'll be able to reach my little sister. She'll see to my wounds and ready us a way out."

"A sister! Hah — you've got a sister. That's fitting, and an absolute tragedy."

Perhaps she did not trust him; and if she did, that was more repellent still — but either way, what Elsa told him let Subaru reckon a way through the fix he was in. Their flight had already carried them a fair distance south; it would not take long to move to the place she meant. With the holy gold coins Elsa carried, Subaru bought the slum's residents into fouling the guards' search and sending them down the wrong roads. No trouble at all.

"What I don't understand is what you're after in this."

"Only to put you in my debt. Might come in handy, having you around one day."

"A debt. How strange — when you want so badly to kill me."

That was what Subaru had to hear while he lent Elsa his shoulder and helped her flee. Her dark eyes turned up at him, feeling for the thing behind his own — though she had already struck it dead center, and had no need to feel at all. The answer she read in his dark eyes was exactly the one she had spoken aloud.

Subaru would have killed Elsa there and then, had it been in his power. But he could not best even a dying Elsa without first weighing every branching outcome, and besides, it was a rash thought. He felt he had been set down on a board tilted hideously against him — a game that assumed he would consider every possible method, work through his trial and error, and play each move for the best. Think of it as shogi: no amount of shoving pieces about at random buys you a single victory. Trade enemies for allies where you can; where an opening shows itself, do not hesitate to remake your own circumstances. For that, he would use even those he loathed and would sooner have killed.

"I do want to kill you. And I will kill you, one day. Just not now."

"I see."

It was not even worth concealing, and so he laid the truth of his heart bare before her. A more careful Elsa would have killed him on the spot, to spend one future worry now. But Subaru was certain she would not — a certainty steeped in blood, bought with better than eighty deaths at her hands.

"Wonderful. You and I are bound together by loathing. One day — yes. You'll prove yourself right. And that is very, very lovely."

"—"

Her blood-wet lips loosened into a smile, the smile of a girl in the grip of a first crush. With that smile close at his side, Subaru thought, from the very floor of his heart: *repulsive.*

***

"Let's meet again, shall we?"

"Bye, mister. Thanks for helping Elsa out."

The blue-haired girl they met up with took Elsa off his hands, and Subaru let out a long breath. The child waiting in the hovel Elsa had named was very young — no more than her early tweens. Her age startled him at first, but she dressed Elsa's wounds with a practiced hand, made their arrangements to slip out of the Capital without delay, and emptied the shack in short order. He managed, only just, to secure a means of reaching Elsa again, which stood as his payment — though whether that payment truly answered for the danger he'd run was another matter.

"Guess what comes of all this depends on how I play it from here."

Subaru peeled off the bloodstained jersey, knotted it around his waist, and set off — away from the slums, toward the Loot House. Elsa had never once reached the place; but had Satella managed to take back her insignia safely? The question gnawed at him.

"—"

After a time he came to the Loot House, and his eyes went wide at the sight of it.

"Well. Didn't see that coming."

What stood before him was a Loot House frozen solid — or rather, sealed inside ice, which came nearer the truth than frozen. What on earth had happened here? Subaru caught hold of a passing resident and pressed him.

"Old boss of that loot house and his granddaughter, the guards hauled 'em both off. Something about crossing some frightening magician… me, I don't want a thing to do with it."

"The old man and the granddaughter — are they alive? And the magician?"

"Word is nobody got hurt, but I didn't get a proper look at either of 'em. Look — that's enough, all right?"

Unnerved, perhaps, by the eerie intensity in Subaru, the man pushed his arms away and made off briskly into the dark of an alley. Subaru turned the words over as he watched him go, then laid a hand to his chest and let his relief out. There was no telling how true any of it was; but at the least the account could not be mistaken for the kind in which people had died. That the guards had taken Felt and Rom was only to be expected, given the trade they were in. Let them enjoy a stretch of prison, and think a while on their line of work. And now that he knew Satella was safe, as he'd hoped —

"—Right. So what do I do now?"

Subaru scratched his head, aware that he had run clean out of goals. Summoned to another world, handed Return by Death, he had used it, and saved an adorable, kindhearted, silver-haired half-elf — though it had cost him eighty-seven deaths to do it.

"Ah, hell. Maybe I should've just died, reset, and gotten Elsa to tell me why she wanted Satella's insignia in the first place…?"

He really ought to have asked while he was carrying the wounded Elsa, but he'd let it slip. Then again, had he asked questions that reached too deep into Elsa's business, and had that led her to see that his heart lay on Satella's side, there was no guessing how she would have taken it. In the end, both he and Satella were alive. Call it the correct answer and be done.

"Still — I'd give a lot to know more about Satella, if I could…"

Where had Satella come from? Where was she going? Would he see her again? He did not know. He could always repeat things until he did know, of course. But supposing there were some other way —

"—So. Would you all mind teaching me?"

Standing before the frozen Loot House, Subaru pushed his hands into his pockets and turned around. Not a soul felt present. And yet his eyes made out several figures standing there — perfectly ordinary-looking people, a scattering of ages and sexes, a group with no coherence to it whatever. If anything about them did cohere, it was the eyes. Every last pair of them was dead. Well suited to being steeped in madness, and to the chase after some crazed ecstasy. And Subaru suspected that, were he to find a mirror, his own eyes would look no different. His cheeks eased at the thought, and he lifted his face to the sky, where a moon of eerie luster looked down — on the frozen Loot House, and on the madmen.

***

It was very nearly two months later that Subaru learned more of Satella.

"In the Royal Selection — a silver-haired half-elf — backed by Margrave Roswaal L. Mathers — not Satella. *Emilia!*"

Falling upon this sudden flood of particulars after so long a silence, Subaru clapped his hands, the picture of joy. The windfall had come of an official proclamation out of the Royal Palace, carrying word not only through the Capital but across the whole country of the contest for the throne. When he'd first seen the bulletins going up around town he had sneered at them as some idiot country election — and then his eyes had flown open. The candidates were five women. And one of them was the girl he had been searching for.

"The Royal Selection, the Royal Selection… if she stands as a candidate for ruler, she's pedigree. Of course she is — think how graceful she is only walking down a street. Emilia. She's Emiliaaa…"

To learn her true name set his heart light, as though it had grown wings. He had worked out long ago that Satella was a borrowed name, and worked out, too, why she — Emilia — had given him a false one. For if a silver-haired half-elf named herself Satella —

"People would take her for one of us and keep their distance. It was a last, desperate stroke to hold me clear of danger. That adorable face of hers, those adorable thoughts of hers."

Speaking so sadly of being a half-elf, looking every moment as if she feared to be turned away, she had still thought first of others, and turned even her own bloodline into a tool for keeping harm at arm's length from them. What a noble, pitiable girl. It wrung his chest to the point of pain.

When —

"—Natsuki Subaru! Beloved of the disciples! Are you *present!*"

"—"

A shrill voice, like a fowl throttled mid-cry, went grating through the room, calling his name. Subaru grimaced, set the bulletin he'd filched from the Capital down on his bed, and went, with no enthusiasm at all, to open the door and step out. He leaned back against it once it was shut, waiting — and down the ill-lit hallway there came into view a man of a sickly, bloodless pallor.

"I had been *seeking you!* Why — why — why should you be here, indulging yourself in *sloth?* When we must follow the Witch's auspices, and answer her love with *diligenceeeeeeeee!*"

"Lay off the false charges, Pete-san. I'm only doing what the gospel tells me. The gospel says to spend my time here."

"*What!* The gospel instructed you thus? At this hour, at this opening, in these very straits — what conceptions must the Witch hold, to bid a disciple as devout as you sit idle — the barest guess at it lies beyond *my faculties!*"

It was not only the screaming that grated; the man's every movement was an offense in itself. A madman through and through, this Petelgeuse Romanée-Conti. It took real effort not to sigh in his face. An unlovable lunatic he was — but, for Subaru's purposes, a serviceable one. Everyone in the fellowship Subaru had attached himself to was a portrait of self-serving ego, and it was an open question whether the rank-and-file disciples possessed any self-awareness at all. Subaru's own idle guess was that the dullness of their feeling might be the work of something laid over their minds, so that their allegiance would not leak out of them while they went about ordinary life. But guesswork could keep. What mattered was what Petelgeuse — who stood in an authority quite unlike the rank and file — meant to get from this visit. Two months Subaru had belonged to the group now, and on the whole the living had been tolerable.

Tolerable to live, that was; for the nerves and the sanity it was a nightmare. Steady dealings with madmen and religious fanatics were, for a man whose own humanity could not rise above the commonplace, simply agony. His lodging was a cave hidden in the mountains — surprisingly habitable, hospitable enough that he could carve out a corner and call it "his room," but the distance from any town, the hard floors, the cold walls, were things this thoroughly modern boy had to grit his teeth and bear. He was not, however, actor enough to keep his ties to the group hidden; so, as a compromise, he lived here, and made himself useful learning and hoarding away everything he could of this world.

"So — you've got some business with a hermit like me?"

"Are you *not aware* — of the foolish affair now being conducted in this country!"

"Something happening in this country'd be… you mean the Royal Selection?"

"*Indeed!* The Selection! And yet — the trouble does not lie *there!* The Selection itself is of no account; it is the one who takes part in it! The silver-haired half-witch!"

The better to instruct the ignorant Subaru, Petelgeuse produced the official notice of the Selection — the very same sheet Subaru had pinned up about his room — and stabbed a finger at it. On the flyer, of course, was the face of the girl Subaru had all but bored holes through with staring; and Petelgeuse's bony finger, just as one would expect, came to rest on her.

"*Behold!* This visage! This stock! Twin blasphemies against the Witch! This creature is not one we may suffer to overlook! The hour of the Trial is *upon us!*"

"The Trial."

"*Precisely!*"

Screeching it, Petelgeuse slapped the flyer flat to the cave wall and drove his fist into the little printed description of Emilia, spraying blood across the stone. The self-savaging madman, drunk on his own pain and his own blood, had defiled her paper likeness.

"If she coheres — take her! If she does not — cast her aside! Should she be proven a fitting vessel for the Witch, we shall gather her into *our fold!* The Trial must begin!"

"And you're asking me to help."

"Yes — *just so!* I have reached out to others, though I doubt those faithless ones will answer. *Wrath* alone might hold some promise — yet she is far from this land at present. And so! We two shall set out alone!"

Duty in his heart and a torrent of tears down his face, Petelgeuse crammed the bloody fist into his mouth and sucked at the wounds; the corners of his mouth split where his teeth bore down, tearing at his own skin and flesh in a display gruesome past enduring. But Subaru ordered himself to weather the sight with iron will, and kept his cool.

"Mind if I come with you, then? I don't know the particulars, Sin Archbishop."

"So you are *kindly accompanying!* Aaah — *aaAaAa!* How very, how very, how very, how very, veryveryveryveryveryvery… *joyous!*"

"That's a bit much to work yourself into when all I said was that I'd tag along."

The wry smile Subaru gave was an honest one. After nodding until his head all but came loose, Petelgeuse snapped his legs back into a proper stance and spun about, his back to Subaru.

"We *depart at once!* I gave my Fingers their instructions along the road here… we shall rejoin with them, and make for the Mathers Domain. And there — we follow where the *gospel leads!*"

"Got it. …And, hey — what is the Trial, exactly?"

"A *sound question!* It is a rite to determine the fitness of the Witch's vessel — whether the receptacle holds the strength, the quality, and above all the qualifications to house the *Witch's soul!*"

Subaru answered the unhelpful account with a knowing nod. He grasped none of the specifics. But set beside the agony of those days when he had known nothing at all of Satella-who-was-Emilia, it was beyond all measure preferable. And it seemed this meant he would come near Emilia once again. Though it also meant —

"—Now — now, now! Now, now, now! We *depart!* We *put her to the test!* Should the vessel prove fit, then upon us comes the Witch's *advent!* A chance, after all these centuries, to fill every one of our empty seats!"

"If the Witch Satella comes down into her — then the vessel…?"

"*Martyred!* And yet — how *venerable!* So venerable that I would take the office myself, were it only possible! Could this body of mine but suit Satella's spirit, I would endure every iteration of unmeasurable suffering, for the wish to *see her again!*"

"So she gets erased. Does she, now."

Subaru murmured it, trailing after Petelgeuse, who had already begun to walk. It never reached the ears of the cackling madman, sealed off as he was in a world of his own — that faint, quiet murmur —

"Oh. Does she, now."

— nor did the madman mark the dark smile on Natsuki Subaru's face.

***

He drove the sword into the thin body and gave it a twist. The tremor coming back up the blade told him he had cut through something vital. And, on a faint rasp of breath —

"You won't believe me when I say this, but—"

"Wh… y…?"

"I thought you were one of the better ones, Pete-san. Out of a whole crowd of unlikable people."

The wide eyes looked down into Subaru's own, despairing. The surprise stayed fixed in them as Petelgeuse tipped slowly backward and Subaru drew the sword out of his chest, the pull of it making him stagger a pace. He breathed in deep. At his feet lay Petelgeuse Romanée-Conti in a spreading pool of blood — Petelgeuse, dying, his heart ruined by Subaru's own hand.

"It was real work, setting this whole thing up. For all the raving you do, you're a little too meticulous. Honestly — I got stuck more times than I can count."

"What… are… *you… saying?*"

"All the trial and error it took to get here. Planning the strategy was part of it, sure, but getting my trump card into place — that was touch and go the whole way. You've no idea how relieved I am right now."

Petelgeuse crawled, forcing strength into limbs that had none left, unable to rise, only creeping backward as though he might retreat out of death's reach. Subaru had nothing left to do. The man would not last long.

"Couldn't even see the Unseen Hand, at first — I was in a real panic over what the hell was happening. Your Fingers gave me no end of grief, either. Right now… I feel like I've actually done something."

"Ghh — auhh…"

The blood running out of Petelgeuse was the last of his life, and rather than slacken it only came the harder, while Subaru stood over him and laid the whole thing bare, the way a mastermind does. The black, invisible Unseen Hand. The Fingers — his confidants, his reserve stock of bodies. And the Possession that let him migrate into them and stretch out his life. With all three worked to their fullest, Petelgeuse had been the very portrait of his beloved diligence in hounding Emilia toward her end. Bringing him down had been a back-breaking labor — better than four hundred rounds of trial and error, in fact, to reach this point.

"Which makes you the person I've talked to most in all the world. I know how insanely lopsided that is — but I feel like you're something close to a friend. Watching you throw down every card you had to reach what you were after… I'll admit it moved me."

"What are you saying — what are you saying, what are you saying, areyousaying, areyousaying—!"

At the very last of the last, a livid strength flooded the moribund body. Pouring all of it into himself, Petelgeuse hauled his dying frame upright. He spat blood — literally, it came welling out of his mouth — his bloodshot eyes flying wide as he wheeled on Subaru.

"Betrayer! Traitor, who spurns the Witch's love! You cannot be — you cannot be — *forgiven!*"

He flung out a blood-drowned hand. Not to call up the Unseen Hand; there was no use to an invisible arm once it could be seen. What Petelgeuse was reaching for was the one condition under which an evil spirit — Petelgeuse Romanée-Conti — could seize hold of another's body: he could possess those with the grounding of a spiritualist, and there was only one person present who answered to that —

"Your body — is—!"

*Mine.* So Petelgeuse meant to finish; but Subaru only sighed. He walked over at his leisure and drove a kick into the dying face, the force of it knocking teeth loose, and Petelgeuse reeled back, stunned. He had not been able to cross into the body he'd thought his for the taking. Subaru's answer came not in words but on his raised left hand — where, above his fingers, there floated a dim red light. A minor spirit: a creature that had entered into a contract with Natsuki Subaru, who had the grounding of a spiritualist, a small dear tool for lighting dim paths. The evil spirit Petelgeuse could take only the bodies of spiritualists who had no such contract. How many deaths it had cost Subaru to work that one out, there was no saying.

"Longest three days of my life, these. Though from where you're standing, I suppose we hardly knew each other at all…"

"*Natsuki Subaruuuuuu!*"

"You went after Emilia. —Regret it."

Petelgeuse spat out a shriek of pure hatred, and Subaru kicked him in the chest and brought the sword down across his face. The blade sank into the skull, wrecking the brain and the life inside it, and the grating death-wail cut off. Subaru leaned his weight down onto the sword where it stood in the man, and sighed. Very short in fact, and yet endless by his own reckoning, the fight with Petelgeuse was over. Something like accomplishment and something like desolation came flooding into him together.

"Mm-hm? Looks like you're finished too."

After a stretch of silence a voice spoke to him. He turned to find a huge shape slinking through the brush — a black-pelted beast with a lion's head and four dreadful limbs, coming toward him. The one who had spoken was not the beast, of course, but the girl astride its back, throwing him a coy little glance.

"Yeah. All done. Thanks, Meili."

"Don't mention it. You're paying us, and you looked after Elsa. But — are you sure about this? I thought that lot were your friends."

"Good question. If he hadn't tried to kill me right there at the end, maybe I'd have called us friends with a bit of an age gap. But he did try, so… disqualified from friendship, I'm afraid."

Wiping the spattered blood off with his sleeve, Subaru shrugged at the girl, and Meili set a finger to her chin.

"Hmmm. I'm not trying to kill you — so does that mean we're friends?"

"By that logic, yeah. You and me are friends, Meili."

"Yay! And if I count Petra-chan and everyone too, then I've got lots of friends!"

She clasped her hands and rocked her shoulders happily where she sat on the beast. The childishness of it made Subaru lift a brow — and the claim to friends left him openly startled.

"Huh. Color me surprised. It's a hell of a thing for me of all people to say, but — you've actually got friends."

"I doo. I killed them all, though."

"—"

Meili said it with a small smile and not the least trace of guilt. Killed them. People she'd been thrown together with through the work, then, most likely. He'd been thinking there was something childlike about her — but there it was, her sense of right and wrong bent all out of true. Well. She was Elsa's sister. What else was there to expect.

"Anyway — it was a serious help, you filling in where I couldn't. Without you I'd never have managed to kill every last one of the Fingers on my own."

"Aw, it's nothing. But — did you really have to go to the trouble of hiring us? You could've asked someone more proper. The Knights, say."

"Couldn't have pulled off part of what I'm after, that way."

"After?"

Meili tipped her head, feeling for what lay behind the word. But Subaru gave nothing more away — only shaped a smile and said, "The rest of this is grown-up talk. A child like you has no need to hear it, Meili."

"Ahh — geez, there you go treating me like a kid! I don't care about you anymore, mister. I'm done. I don't caaare!"

She flung it at him in a temper, and the beast growled as though her mood had lit a fuse in it. Setting her pet on him. After all the pains he'd gone to at last to kill Petelgeuse, he'd really rather this attempt didn't end in failure now. So Subaru set himself to patching up Meili's mood, and toiled away, for all he was worth, as her flatterer.

***

A few days into the Royal Selection, a considerable change came over the territories of Lugnica. The largest of them was that the front-runner for the throne, Duchess Crusch Karsten, fell out of the running — though the only one who understood the whole truth of it was Subaru. For every trace of this "Crusch" had been wiped out of the world, struck away together with the very fact that she had ever existed, the world revised so that she had never been there at all. Which meant the Selection had now always been a contest of four; the memories of Duchess Karsten's own supporters had been reworked, and they had drifted into the service of other camps.

"God, the White Whale's fog is a terror. Going around erasing the record of a whole person like that."

Why it had no hold on Subaru alone was a mystery. But since he felt nothing of it himself, it amounted to little more than a nuisance — the strange little contradictions that kept surfacing whenever he talked with people. He rather wished he could just forget this wretched Crusch Karsten entirely. A person who had died throwing themselves at an enemy they could never have matched would never be given the chance to come back; the one soul in the world granted that chance was Natsuki Subaru. And so the defeated served no purpose now but as markers: do not do the foolish thing they did.

But there had also been a change that was not, like that one, of concern to no one but Subaru. Namely —

"The Royal Selection candidate Emilia — she who has subjugated the world's long-standing scourge, the Witch Cult's Sin Archbishop of Sloth!"

The Kingdom rang with it; even foreign nations, it seemed, had heard of the feat. Even Subaru was taken aback at how effective a piece of propaganda it made. A victory drawn out of four hundred years of death, claimed by Natsuki Subaru — and handed over, whole and entire, to the shady clown of a man who backed Emilia. How the Margrave would take a proposal to make Emilia the achiever of it had honestly been a gamble on the order of subjugating Petelgeuse itself, but —

"He was so easy about it it turned my stomach."

Margrave Roswaal L. Mathers had heard Subaru's proposal out, agreed to it without the least fuss, and announced at once that the felling of Petelgeuse was Emilia's doing. Subaru felt there was something not quite right about the man — yet he had no quarrel with what Roswaal did. This world reviled Emilia for stupid, baseless reasons. And still Roswaal had declared himself her backer, taken her under his patronage, and pressed her forward into the Selection. Perhaps it was mere caprice; more likely he was angling to profit should the one-in-a-million chance come off and Emilia take the throne. The way he had swooped in, all but drooling, at Subaru's report on Petelgeuse, made proof enough of that.

"No objection here. Do as you please, Mister Margrave. So long as you stand on Emilia's side, I'll throw myself down on your side too. Just as you're counting on — the ruler will be Emilia."

Subaru would spend everything, and his deathless power besides, to see it done. But should it turn out that Roswaal harbored some foul appetite, some unfit thought, where Emilia was concerned — then —

"Then it just means one more gravestone raised for Emilia's sake."

If he was to go on working from the shadows, unknown to Emilia, he had no choice but to leave the public business to Roswaal. In exchange, Subaru would bring every last scheme to fruition from behind the curtain. And to that end —

"Went out of my way to salvage you. I'll be needing your help, Blue."

"—"

At the back of the cave stood a cell of iron bars. Chained within that cold space was someone in filthy clothes: a dainty figure in a knight's uniform, cat's ears rising from the head — a girl, or a young man got up as one, it seemed. While Subaru had been rushing about setting things in order after Petelgeuse's death and the White Whale's rampage, he had come upon this spoil and carried the tool home. As yet, though, he had had precious few chances to witness the healing gifts of the fabled Blue. The man was not even mending his own hurts — only staring at the floor of the cell and weeping without end.

"…Someone, tell me. Tell me. Why am I— where is His Highness? What was I for? There was someone. There had to have been someone. It all makes no sense if there wasn't. But…"

"Yeah. I'm stuck on this one. It's going to take some time."

Subaru scratched his head and drew from his breast pocket a slip of paper — the notice that had announced the opening of the Royal Selection, which he had kept faithfully, unfailingly, on his person. It no longer looked like the thing he knew. Five candidates ought to have been listed there; now there were four, and the descriptions had thinned. The change wrought by the striking-out of Crusch Karsten had reached even this sheet. But Subaru remembered. He might have ignored her utterly, but he had read this thing over more times than he could number, and Crusch Karsten's knight was named Felix Argyle — which ought to be one and the same as this healer here, Blue.

"My guess is that when the Whale's fog erases someone, your memory patches over the hole by changing itself in a way that never feels wrong. But… just look at this."

"Someone, someone tell me… His Highness — His Highness? His Highness, and — someone else…?"

"When the person's so huge you can't patch the hole — I suppose this is what becomes of you."

When the one who has vanished made up so vast a part of who you are, of course you come apart when they drop out of the world. Which was why Blue had ended up murmuring the same broken phrases over and over, like a doll with something snapped inside it. And not even Subaru knew how to mend a mind broken that way. He had never once met the person who had held Blue's heart together; and even were he to go back in time, there was no telling where he would return to, so he did not know the story that had been spun between the two of them.

"Still — I've found myself a fine little pawn here. It's all right. I'll fill in the cracks in your heart, don't you worry."

"Someone — please… tell me. I — why am I…"

Blue gave no sign whatever of hearing him. It might have looked hopeless, but Subaru went on without alarm, prepared to take all the time it wanted. A knight-healer stripped of the one he served — you did not come by so pliable a pawn very often. And so Subaru told him, in deep and honest earnest:

"This time, I'll figure out for certain how to keep you from killing yourself."

*I'm ready to die however many times it takes, matching myself against you,* he swore.

***

Every way into the mansion was boarded up. The doors had been nailed shut from the inside, and planks lay across every window. Had he been sharp enough to find something strange in a house shuttered up so tight, perhaps he might have noticed. Though of course, Subaru had laid the whole thing out in the confidence that he would not.

A raging inferno swallowed the mansion whole, gnawing it steadily down to ash. The fire that started it knew no pause, blazing fiercer and fiercer as it devoured the house, shrieking its frenzied demand that all of it be given back to dust. And it was not the furniture and finery of the burning manor alone that charred. The many women who had passed so long an agony inside these walls lost their lives too, in the embrace of flame and smoke, their shapes crumbling into soot until nothing marked them as ever having been people. A ghastly deed. Anyone would call such savagery by that name. But this was the very thing the burning women had wished for — so one might insist, and who on earth would believe it?

"Damn it, damn it, damn this idiocy—!"

The tongues of flame swelled, driven higher by the wind, and through the burning house rang the sound of a man cursing. His voice was strained to the point of pathos as he screamed inside the collapsing, blazing building — screeched in a perfect frenzy, unable to credit what was happening, with no notion of what in the world had gone wrong.

"Number ninety-nine! Number one-fourteen! Even one-twenty-three would do! Where are you? Where have you run off to? Who do you take me for? Leaving me behind to go off and die on your own — what selfish, faithless women you are!"

His voice cracked as he screamed like a child in the throes of a tantrum, this white-haired young man in white clothing, his ordinary face turned as ghoulish as a demon's in his raving. He made for a very strange sight indeed inside the disaster of the fire. A man in his senses would be doing everything he could to escape the blaze. This one showed not the faintest sign of it — as though the thought that he might die had never once crossed his mind, as though he moved by some creed that stood above mortality itself. He was mad; or rather, he was not. No — it would be wrong to say he was not mad; but the truth was that he was serenely, soundly sure of himself. This fire could not kill him, so he believed. And so all his screaming and cursing had nothing whatever to do with fear for his own life. It came of the boundless rage he bore his wives, who had, in all likelihood, set the fire themselves.

"Every last one of these wretches — taking my finite estate and belittling—"

"—I'd be obliged if you'd close that unpleasant mouth of yours."

"Ngh—"

A foot came crashing in through a blazing wall and caught the frantic man full in the face. Reeling from the angle of the blow no less than from the fact of it, he sailed easily down the corridor; even a burning wall turned brittle, unable to bear the collision, and burst apart against him. He tumbled to a stop sprawled across the floor and stared up at the burning ceiling in blank astonishment.

"What — is—"

"This fire is a parting letter from those many wives of yours. To put it plainly — it seems this is the end of these fetters of love that fear built."

The voice answering the stunned man was the same he'd heard as the kick landed. He jerked himself upright, dragged himself through the shattered wall — and a woman robed in black came stepping into the burning room, her smile resplendent, her long black braid unmistakable. But what spoke her identity plainest of all was the kukri held in her right hand.

"Burglar! Who do you imagine I am? This foolery of yours is what you'll—"

*Regret,* the man was prattling, swinging his arms up to strike at the woman — when a light impact, and the sight of those arms severed at the elbows and spinning through the air, put an end to it. He looked down. Observed that his arms were gone. Impossible. What in the world was happening?

"Immortal? Invincible? I forget which it was — but I know the trick to how it works. All you are now is a very unpleasant little insect of a man."

"—! A slattern like you—"

"—"

Forgetting his lost arms, the man went to curse her; she did not grant him a single word of it. Sweeping her leg up between his own where he sat on the floor, she kicked him square in the crotch, hard enough to launch him into the air, and set her killing blade dancing. She took his arms off at the shoulders, then carved neat rounds of flesh from his legs, from the feet up to the thighs — his toes, his ankles, his shins, his knees, his thighs, all given to the blade, the blood coming in floods as the man's body was swiftly rendered into something past describing.

"—My—"

"It's remarkable that you're still trying to speak, in that state."

She sank her foot into what was left of his torso — a much smaller mark now — and blasted him out through the supposedly boarded window and into the open air. Amid a rain of shattered glass he fell, unable to break the fall with the limbs he no longer had, to the ground. It was his good fortune that it was only the second floor, which spared him a fatal hurt from the drop. Though the lack of limbs and the sheer loss of blood were fatal enough already.

"As if I could bear such abject stupidity. …I am the most perfected being in all the world. Wanting for little, content in my sufficiency, humble and free of avarice — that is how I live my life. So why must I, of all men, suffer the jeering of human failures such as—"

"When you insult people like that, day in and day out, of course you're going to get served the divorce papers, Regulus-san."

"Hauh—!"

Even rolling onto his side was hard labor for the man now, when a new figure cut into his view: a boy of dark hair and dark eyes in a dark robe — Natsuki Subaru. It was pitiful how little the man had grasped of any of it. Subaru sighed down at him.

"I'd never have guessed everyone would pull together so beautifully to bring it off, Regulus-san."

"Why are you — here… no. Then — this was your design?"

"Who else's could it be?"

The man, Regulus, caught up at last with what was happening, as Subaru shrugged, his mouth twisting into a grin. Made small, Regulus's eyes filled with fury.

"Curse you, you scum! Do you comprehend what it is you have done? You took my wives — my beloved wives — and before my very eyes burned them to death along with my house! Do you comprehend the immorality, the wickedness of it? You wife-murdering wretch!"

"That angle's so out of left field I'm honestly lost for words. …Just so you know — your wives gave up their own lives, freely, for the sake of the anti-heart plan."

"—Im… possible."

Subaru dug at his ear, looking genuinely amazed as he delivered the news, and Regulus fell dumb. That Regulus should find it so wholly beyond belief was itself a thing Subaru could not fathom. Regulus had kept a great many women he styled "wives" in his mansion, forever singing the glory of married life while threatening to slaughter any of them who ever crossed him. Had that been the whole of it, it would only have looked like a monstrously cruel sort of harem — but the Sin Archbishop's foulness did not end there. The villain had lodged his own heart inside his wives, and by that made his own body stopped in time, immortal, invincible. The one and only way to kill the Sin Archbishop of Greed, Regulus Corneas, was to give him back his heart — which meant killing every one of the vessels he had chosen to house it, his wives, until it had nowhere left to hide. Even Subaru had agonized over the choice. But it was Regulus's imprisoned wives — no — the imprisoned women who had settled the matter for him.

"They were fine with dying, so long as it paid you back. Even I've never heard of a man's mouth being vile enough to drive people that far."

"Who could possibly believe such nonsense… I — I loved my wives! And so they ought to love me in return! Yes? You don't find it strange to think otherwise? And yet! Why should those damnable women visit such suffering upon me — unfit as wives, the lot of them!"

"…You mean it. That's the terrifying thing about you people."

Subaru muttered it, looking put out, and turned his eyes away from Regulus — and what they came to rest on was a black shape springing from the burning mansion to land in the garden. Elsa. She brushed the soot from herself, then caught his look.

"My, were you worried for me? Set your mind at ease. I wasn't hurt anywhere at all."

"I'm not worried about you. Anyway — what's all this? I never told you to do anything this tasteless to him."

Subaru's mouth bent into a frown as he pointed at the limbless Regulus. Regulus had still had his limbs the last time Subaru happened to see him, so it was Elsa's doing beyond a doubt. She shrugged.

"It seemed to me he'd only be tiresome if he kept them… And wouldn't you rather he was told what they had to say?"

"…Yeah. You've got a point."

It was a surprise to find that Elsa could, after all, show a person some consideration. It would be wrong to let Regulus die without his ever being made to hear how the sacrificed women had steeled themselves as they sprang this trap on him. And now that he had been made to hear it —

"Gghaaaa—AH!"

Elsa ran her kukri into Regulus's chest and used it to lift his light body slowly into the air — for all the world like a morsel skewered on a stick, blood pouring off him as he thirsted, ceaselessly, after life.

"All at once?"

"No…"

Subaru set a hand to his chin and answered her question, thinking. He might be no Elsa, but he had a heart that felt sympathy and felt outrage as much as anyone's did — and that heart was demanding he make some return to the women who had wept and longed for death. And so Subaru gave Elsa her order.

"Throw him into a weaker part of the fire. We'll watch him burn."

"Very well. As you wish."

Elsa nodded at the cruel instruction without a flicker of misgiving, and tossed the cursing Regulus onto a heap of smoldering timber at the edge of the blazing mansion. His body caught, and as he was roasted toward death the man's shriek went ringing up into the night sky. Without the slightest change passing over either of their faces, Subaru and Elsa watched him die.

"An insect, at least, has the grace to soothe you with its noise. He is a good deal worse than that."

When the long, long death-wail had run itself out at last, Elsa offered her verdict. And Subaru, for his part, agreed.

***

To Subaru, the breaking of the glass carried in it the sound of a trust breaking.

"This — is—"

"I'm sorry. I'm so sorry. I never wanted to do this. I truly, truly didn't."

The man's voice shook with shock as he braced an elbow against the counter; but the arm would not hold him, and his upper body slid off the counter's edge, tipping his chair over as he crumpled to the floor of the shop. The glass and bottle came down with him, shattering against the boards and soaking his white uniform with the reek of liquor. His limbs would not answer, and his life ran slowly out of him; his lips faded to purple, his sight swam as he blinked and blinked and blinked, trying to beat back the death coming for him. From his seat at the table, Subaru watched the man's frantic scrabble after life. It was a refined face. The body was lithe, and both his carriage as he'd entered the shop and his choice of words in greeting his friend had brimmed with grace. The knightliest of knights, in truth.

"I can see why they call you the Impeccable, Julius Euclius-san."

"You… cur…"

"But if you'd given a little more thought to your position, you'd have done well to be a bit more mindful of the people around you. You're the knight of a Selection candidate riding the very crest of her popularity. You have to reckon that it isn't only your master who becomes a target — it's you. Though…"

Subaru stood, holding forth over the gasping, agonized Julius, and reached out — not for him, but for the one seated in the chair beside the fallen knight. Flax-brown cat's ears, and features made to be doted on. Subaru drew the dainty shoulders gently in against himself and, condescending to it, patted the head. And by that alone, Blue's eyes softened into a drunken sort of bliss.

"Fer… is that… you…"

"You've no idea what it took to turn his grief to account, you know? I never thought I'd go to such lengths over anyone's life but hers. It paid off, though."

"What… are you… scheming…"

"Nothing to do with you anymore. Set your mind at rest. My reckoning is that with you gone, your master takes no harm, and things go the way she'd want. What happens if she pulls herself together, that I can't say."

Julius's yellow eyes raced through a headlong tangle of it — confusion and rage, sorrow and turmoil, grief and suspicion. Not one of those breakneck feelings meant a thing.

"An old friend calls you out for a drink, and on the first swallow, this. Trust is a sweet poison, Mister Impeccable. You drank yourself under on it, and you miscalculated."

"…oi. Son."

"Must be nice, being someone the whole world's proud of. A life that easy — I could almost envy it. You're dead, though."

Subaru crouched and looked into Julius's face. The man was not even looking at him. He wasted no last thought on the one who had ordered his death. What lay in his eyes was his fear for his friend, made to do murder against his will — and his contrition toward the master who was not there to see it.

"—"

"A knight to the very last. I despise it."

Subaru spat the words down at Julius's dead face, silent now forever. The sickness rising in his chest came, most likely, of the fact that he had had no need whatever to kill the Knight Impeccable. This was very nearly the first time Subaru had ordered a death without a reason for it. It had not pained him to see cultists or Sin Archbishops die; but this killing came purely of Subaru's having chosen the easier of two prey to torment. Julius was less guarded than Anastasia Hoshin, who was forever ringed round with knights — and, more to the point, the Blue whom Subaru had coaxed into dependence could be used against him.

"Did I do well, Subaru-sama?"

"You did wonderfully. I'm sorry I forced it on you."

Blue asked it quietly, standing at his side as Subaru saw Julius's death through, and Subaru shrugged and gave him another pat. If this much was enough to settle that unmoored mind, then he would pat him as many times as it took.

"It's all right, so long as it's of use to you, Subaru-sama. This was needed to shape the future you and His Highness are wishing for. Wasn't it? Wasn't it?"

"Yeah. It was. That's why we couldn't let your friend go on living."

"Yes…"

Blue's face was drained of feeling, and yet his friend's death seemed to crack something in his mind; he caught at Subaru's sleeve as though to fill the hollow it had opened in his heart. The smell of death hung heavy on the air. When —

"—Oh. Am I intruding?"

The door of a place no one had any business entering swung open, and a delicate-looking man stepped into the shop. For an instant Subaru did think of silencing the witness; but there was no need to silence this one. He was an insider.

"Right on time."

"I am. Time is a finite thing, and we merchants hold that time is money."

"Still calling yourself a merchant — the nerve. You're more of a death-dealer."

"To that, I confess, I have no answer at all."

It was a thin, grey-haired man who scratched at his head in reply. In his sleek black suit and black tie he might have passed for someone come from a funeral; but a closer look turned up on him the scent of death that could not be got rid of. His features were gentle, and so were his eyes — yet the way he ran his gaze around the room betrayed a plain wariness of others, and told that he had come through slaughters and lived. Most of all, though, Subaru liked those dreary eyes: the eyes of a man who had found no profit in staying alive, who had lost the whole point of living and had gone on living anyway, an animate corpse. Eyes of a kind with Blue's.

"Your name — don't believe I ever caught it."

"I have never given it, and I don't intend to. Nor, naturally, have I any wish to ask yours, esteemed client. This way, the two of us can both breathe easy."

"Fair enough. It's not as if we could ever be friends."

"Just so. We'd become enemies the very instant anything went wrong. And is this before us not exactly what comes of calling such people friends?"

The man said it lightly, his eye going to Julius's sprawled corpse, and to the Blue who clung so close against Subaru. Both the getting of the poison and the cleaning-up afterward were things Subaru had arranged hand in hand with this man — for just now, Subaru was walking some very perilous tightropes.

"Give Russell my thanks. I'll be counting on you again. You are his right hand, after all."

"That man's right hand is at the end of his own arm. What I am is a slave."

*He reasons in clean, tidy lines,* Subaru thought of the realist's reply, with something like fondness — and with something like regret. If only they could have been friends. They would surely have made good ones.

Again and again Subaru ran his secret operations, whittling away at the candidates and at the Sin Archbishops who stood in his path. A strange fellowship, a nest of dangerous people — and, more valuable than any of that, a place where no one troubled overmuch to interfere with anyone else. The Witch Cult had made a very convenient masquerade for Subaru. A masquerade he would, in the end, have to snuff out, yes; but to use them with that end already fixed in mind meant he could cast them off, when the time came, with a lack of guilt that was almost refreshing. He felt not the faintest kinship even for the Sin Archbishops. He watched them, always, for openings; he turned over the ways of killing them, tested them, and where a plan felt promising, laid the groundwork for it. And even if he failed, Subaru had the power to reset the world and try again.

Even Subaru, placed where he was, had come to form ties that might, perhaps, be called partnerships — though he knew there was not one cultist among them who ought to be trusted, and that he had no business drawing more people in.

"But haven't we been in this together the whole way, ever since the start? It makes me happy, you know, when you let me lend a hand with all the fun things, mister."

"I've no particular intention of supporting you without condition; and even now, you're thinking that you'll kill me the moment the chance comes. For some reason, I find that truly delightful."

"His Highness's dream comes true so long as you're here, Subaru-sama. And so I'll stay beside you, always. …But — hm? Subaru-sama, when — where was it that you and His Highness first became…"

"What lies between us is surely nothing but jobs of dirty work. Partners? What an idiotic notion. The pair of us are unfilial bankrupts who can't show our faces to our own families. It'd be pleasanter to die, if only I could."

"I've come far enough that one step — just one step more — stands between me and the granting of the wish. And iiiiif that is what I do it for, then I'll sell my soul to a demon. A demon by the name of you — thaaat is what I mean."

—Subaru did not, for a moment, think what he did was worthy of praise. And yet, before he could so much as notice it, there had come to be people who stood by his deeds and gave them their blessing. Perhaps that fact was his one consolation. Using the power of Return by Death, Natsuki Subaru struggled onward, wishing to save Emilia. He had believed the fight would be a lonely one — that he would always, only ever, be fighting on his own. And that at some point he could not name it had stopped being lonely was, perhaps —

***

An inferno swallowed the Capital. Subaru gasped with pain, staring up at the sky. He felt very much awake. The pain stung — but it drove his mind toward clarity rather than away from it, and everything, from what had happened to the reason he was here, stood vivid before him. In his arms Subaru cradled a motionless Meili. Her open eyes were empty of life, fixed on some point that was nowhere in this world. That cheeky manner of hers, that sugary voice so ill-matched to it, the way she'd thrown a child's tantrum whenever she was treated like a child — none of it would he ever see again.

For this, after all, was where Subaru had arrived on Returning by Death. The point to which Return by Death carried him could shift, but never once had it sent him back further than his last such point. This was where the spawn had moved to; and since the Meili in his arms was already a corpse, her life lay past all saving. Knowing full well that he was only soothing his own heart, Subaru gently closed Meili's eyes. He had no right to pray for any peace beyond death for her. These hands of his were far too fouled with blood for that. And it was the same for Meili herself: she had done so much wrong that she could not hope for rest on the other side. Sin heaped upon sin, the chains that bound Subaru and his people to hell would never come loose.

"But — even so…"

A meeting with death was no cause to give it up. Had he not come this far by dying, over and over? A thousand times, ten thousand times, he had already cheapened life and defiled lives, greedy to see his own wish granted, and so reached this place. Times without number he had come near to breaking under the agony of dying. But each time he did, a fire scorched his heart — that very first inferno, the touch of those fingers, was the thing that had carried Subaru this far. He stood one step, only one, from the desire at his very core. He had made victims, made sacrifices, to arrive here at last. And still —

"—Come no further."

The voice came down, savage, from above. *An inferno swallows the Capital* had been no figure of speech. The figure that appeared atop the hill, as though it were gathering the raging blaze into itself, was a dazzling thing to see.

"Reinhardt. Van. Astreaaa…"

"It seems I've no need to introduce myself. Nor have I much to say to you."

Reinhardt's blue eyes ran the kneeling Subaru clean through. The light in them was nothing like what Subaru had seen so long ago — they were fraught now with feeling. This man, who had kept his calm in the very face of a butcher, looked upon Subaru with an emotion so fierce it all but beggared description: he glared at him with hatred.

"So you could hate people after all, Sword Saint!"

"It surprises me as well. I hadn't thought such feelings were in me."

"So you've found a new self. Congratulations. Many happy returns, Reinhardt."

"Unfortunately, today is not the day of my birth. But it will be the day of your death."

Let Subaru rattle off all the affected lines he liked; a man of Reinhardt's caliber would not be thrown by them. Struck with Reinhardt's own brand of death sentence, a smile rose to Subaru's face. He had ruined that unshakeable face, and drawn out the feeling buried under it. It was, Subaru knew, utterly without point — and it was the one victory he could claim.

"Don't make me laugh, Reinhardt! Sword Saint! Sword of the Kingdom! A knight who guards the Kingdom of Lugnica, are you? Have you guarded any kingdom at all? Suppose you tell me that!"

"—"

Subaru shouted it with his arms flung wide, spit flying. The two of them faced each other outside the palace gates, and everything of the Capital that could be seen from that height was wrapped in fire — no, and not the Capital alone. This inferno was raging across the whole territory of the Kingdom of Lugnica. However strong Reinhardt was, however peerless a knight, what could one man do against it? Nothing. So Subaru had concluded.

"This is my gift to you! A trap I laid to kill you!"

"To kill me…?"

"How many times — how many times do you think I've tried to kill you? Tens, hundreds, thousands of times — how many times do you think I've stood against you?"

"—"

Subaru's incoherent shouting stiffened Reinhardt's cheeks with confusion. He wouldn't understand. No one but Subaru ever would. Subaru had already worked through every conceivable form of trial and error to kill Reinhardt. He had studied Reinhardt van Astrea, researched him to exhaustion, tried out every idea he could raise, adjusted his plans, run down every avenue his mind could reach in the attempt to end him. And no matter what method Subaru used, Reinhardt overcame it — as though Natsuki Subaru's hideously feeble brain, as though his very existence, could not touch the man in the slightest. He had sacrificed Elsa, sacrificed Meili, sacrificed Blue, sacrificed the man who might have been his friend, sacrificed the clown who called himself his co-conspirator, sacrificed the Witch Cult, sacrificed the Sin Archbishops, committed every crime and wrong and iniquity a person could conceive — and still could not kill Reinhardt. And so, having no way to cut Reinhardt's lifeline, Subaru had made his decision.

"I'm killing the *you* that is a knight. I'm dragging that showy name of yours, Sword Saint, down into the dirt — and stamping all over it, and spitting on it, and worse!"

"All of this. Only for that."

"Only for that! Yes, I did it — only for that! For that alone I spent every life there was, to drag you down into the mud!"

Subaru laid Meili's body down on the ground. Leveled a finger at Reinhardt. And Reinhardt could not hide that he was shaken. It felt good.

"You're a hero, Reinhardt. It isn't possible for me to kill you. But a hero — a hero I can kill. This is how one kills you, Reinhardt."

"—"

Subaru declared it, his voice come unhinged, at the silenced Reinhardt. He had labored with everything in him, tried plan upon plan, sacrificed people, spilled blood, stacked death upon death, and at last — with Meili having shielded him and stretched out his survival — at last, Subaru had managed to stand before Reinhardt like this. After so great a pile of sacrifices, he had managed, finally, to stand upon the same stage as the man.

"…Why?"

The hot wind blew the momentum out of him, and when he spoke again his voice was frail. "Why are you so strong? Why so strong that I had to let them all die, only to match you?"

His voice shook, threaded through with sobs, and hearing it stiffened Reinhardt's face. The man could have no idea in the world what Subaru was thinking. Of course he couldn't. Subaru no longer had any idea himself. He did not know why he was weeping. How long had it even been since the last time he'd shed a tear? Surely — surely that day, the day he was brought to this world, had been the last. The tears he had wept on the day that led Natsuki Subaru to this sea of flame had been his last.

"I wish I'd been like you. I wish I'd been honest, like you — strong enough to save everyone, like you. I think you're to be envied. I think you're to be despised."

"You—"

Out with his tears came the truth of Natsuki Subaru's heart. Back in that very first loop through the Capital, when Natsuki Subaru had watched the man who freed him from a cycle without end — back then, Subaru had been jealous of Reinhardt. Cut open times beyond counting, made to die and die, and still unable to change a single thing, Subaru's heart had frayed fast, the skin of it peeling raw; and then, all but by accident, as easily as flicking a pebble off the road, the man had turned that fate aside. Subaru had longed for such strength, had envied it, resented it, hated it.

"—I wanted to be you, Reinhardt."

"—I don't understand you."

Reinhardt cast Subaru's honesty aside as worthless drivel. And he was right. Reinhardt — you hero — you are always, always right.

Where had the wrong turn been? At what point had Natsuki Subaru gone astray? He didn't know. Though in truth — he did. But no one alive would be able to understand it. Which was why — which was precisely why — Natsuki Subaru had long since become a madman no one could feel with.

"—"

Reinhardt narrowed his eyes and lowered himself. He did not reach for a sword. Which meant that not even he believed a weapon was needed to put Subaru down. And he was right — entirely right. One blow from him would leave Subaru's flimsy body scattered to nothing. So at the very least — at the absolute, uttermost least —

"—Isn't it a little soon to be giving up?"

"You—!"

The instant Reinhardt broke into his charge, a black shape swept in from the side. A jarring clangor rang out as Reinhardt met the shape's slash with his hand, held flat and open like a blade. It was absurd. How could a man take a knife swung with such force on his bare hand? The knife shrieked and burst to pieces. It was absurd. Why was it the knife that broke?

"You really are something outside the ordinary run of things, aren't you."

Elsa, drenched in blood, spun through the air and came down on her hands and feet. Subaru had thought her dead. He had believed he'd spent her as a piece on the board to make this moment, believed her life burned away to nothing, and had been acting on that belief. And yet.

"It seems I didn't die after all. Though, in a place like this, it feels rather as though I've come to call on death a second time."

"Elsa…"

"Then Meili is dead. My poor little sister."

Marking Meili where she lay on the ground, Elsa murmured it with a shade of sorrow. But she changed her gears in the space of a blink and turned to face Reinhardt squarely.

"It appears you're my sister's enemy as well. Shall we dance?"

"You've already lost your weapon. And do you understand just whom it is you're protecting?"

"Difficult subjects were never to my taste. I do the things I want to do; and the man behind me lets me do the things I want to do. That makes him a prized customer of mine."

Licking her lips, Elsa gave an answer grounded in the sort of logic only murderers would follow. Reinhardt drew a breath, and set himself to take her for his opponent.

"This will be the last chance I get for it. I've had fun. It was wonderful."

"Elsa — I—!"

"Farewell."

True to herself to the end, the parting came smooth and easy, with no regard whatever for anything Subaru might have wished. And on its heels Elsa sprang, in a dance of animal grace, at Reinhardt, hero and butcher closing in furious combat — Elsa Granhiert's last meal of blood, to be finished in the briefest of times.

"—Damn it!"

Subaru could not die here. Wrenching his eyes from Elsa's fight and from Meili's corpse, he broke into a run down the hill, the overspill of the battle ringing out behind him, growing distant.

Buildings folded one after another throughout the blazing Capital, screams and wails going up through this hell. Children crying for parents, parents crying for children, men crying for women and women for men, in the shrieking bedlam of it. Yes. This was the hell that Subaru had made. He had made this hell, shattered the false image of Reinhardt, and reached his goal. All that remained now was —

"Y-you—"

*"—ssssss."*

Subaru stumbled, all but pitched forward onto his face — when a huge beast caught him up in its jaws and flung him onto its back. He clung on for dear life as the black pelt filled his sight, and with it the ferocious, lion-like visage of the thing as it ran.

"You're… but Meili's already—dead…"

It was one of the Witchbeasts Meili had commanded, carrying Subaru at a dead run through the burning Capital. The ferocious Guiltylawe sprinted on with Natsuki Subaru upon his back. He had no master now, and no obligation to obey the man — and still Guiltylawe bore Natsuki Subaru along, with all his heart.

"Please — find her. She has to be somewhere…"

Subaru prayed, wishing for Guiltylawe to work some miracle. What was he to call this thing in his heart? Relief? Resignation? Bound about by feelings he could not name, Natsuki Subaru's mind swung between waking and the dark. But what truly brought him to his limit was what came next.

"—That's enough of that."

The sharp lance of ice ran sprinting Guiltylawe through the flank. The beast shrieked, tripped over his own feet, and went crashing to the ground; caught in the fall, Subaru too was slammed against the stone of the road.

"Ghau… what — was—"

His sight blurred with pain as he forced his eyes open and looked round to see what had happened. Guiltylawe had hauled himself upright, a countless press of icicles buried in his left side, facing off against a figure wrapped in dim light who stood on the very edge of a charge. Guiltylawe lifted a savage paw and roared — his dignity, perhaps, as a Witchbeast, or his pride as the last animal that had obeyed young Meili to the end. His blow was more than strong enough to tear a human being to shreds, should it land. But before it could, a single sharp icicle came plunging down — into Guiltylawe's open maw, driving down his throat, then out through his chest and clean through his hindquarters, turning him too into a beast run through on a spit. Hard on the heels of that ruinous but not-quite-mortal blow, the air rang and crackled as white frost spread over the whole of Guiltylawe's body, and made of him a statue of ice.

Having watched Guiltylawe's end, Subaru rose slowly to his feet. He could not lift his left arm — dislocated, perhaps, in the fall. It ought to have hurt enough to break him into tears; but his brain would not register it. For if Subaru began to bawl now, here, it would ruin everything.

"—That's enough, villain."

The two of them faced each other, the burning townscape of the city to either side and a frozen Witchbeast between. Amethyst eyes ablaze with duty and outrage met dark eyes brimming with a joy that would not be held down. Silver hair the hue of the moon, eyes of finest-cut amethyst; her heavenly face set Subaru's heart racing without end, and the silver chime of her voice reached him like the aria of a fairy. What he had chased, sought, craved, never once stopped loving — her face — was here.

"—Emilia."

"You know me?"

Emilia's brows shot up in surprise, and seeing it made Subaru laugh. Exactly as he had pictured her — no. Exactly as she had been in his impression of her, the day they first met and wandered the Capital together. She hadn't the faintest notion how much of the nation's attention was fixed on her, a candidate in the Royal Selection. It was not that she lacked self-awareness; it was that she thought too little of herself — this, though she was known the world over as the hero who had taken down the Witch Cult's Sloth, Greed, Gluttony, Wrath, and Lust, the evils that had tormented people through the ages, and wiped them from the earth. And her record would, this day, this instant, be brought to its completion.

"Is something funny?"

"No — forgive me. It's just, how to put it, I'm happy. It's that feeling, where you — well. You haven't changed in the slightest, so it's as though I've been repaid for everything."

"What do you mean by that? When did you and I ever—?"

Emilia went searching frantically through her memory. But Natsuki Subaru was nowhere to be found in it. Of course not. Their fleeting encounter lived on only in Subaru. And that fleeting encounter alone — that final vow of penitence alone — was what had carried Natsuki Subaru here.

"You're—"

"Lia, it's no use. Don't take a single word he says to heart."

"—Puck, huh."

Emilia was hunting for some thread in her memory when she was cut off by the grey cat that appeared on her shoulder — a spirit, one Subaru remembered well. His memories of that day were far off now. But that was the very reason he had spent still more time turning that day over, endlessly, than he had spent simply recalling it. That he should ever forget one who held a place in that memory was not to be imagined.

"How good of you to call my name so familiarly. With crimes as flamboyant as yours, how do you propose to pay the price enough to settle them?"

"I'll pay it. Exactly the price you want. —There's no way out left for me in any case."

"—? That's rather sporting of you. Suspicious."

Subaru drew down the zip of his tracksuit jacket and spread his arms wide, showing he would not resist. The tracksuit. Yes — the tracksuit. He had taken out the tracksuit he'd kept sealed away until this very day, and put it on. He had settled it in his mind that if he was to have his reunion with Emilia, this was how it ought to be — all the while yearning for the day he would wear this and stand before her again.

"Everything I'm about to tell you is the raving of a madman. Please — don't remember it."

"—Huh?"

"I'm the one who set the Capital alight. And not the Capital only; this fire is meant to burn the whole country down. No one could have guarded against it. Which means the nation, and the knights who guard the nation, have failed to handle the thing."

Subaru's broken, jolting speech threw Emilia into deep confusion. Puck looked torn over whether to cut the talk short, but seeing how it stood with Emilia, he held off his attack. Grateful for it, Subaru went on.

"Reinhardt's standing as Sword Saint has fallen to the earth. And since no one knows what founded the covenant that's supposed to protect the Dragon's Kingdom of Lugnica, the Dragon's no rescue either — I tested that more times than I can count, so it's certainly true. In the end, Reinhardt and the Dragon are one and the same."

"Burning the country? You're — burning the country? To try to destroy this nation?"

"No — wrong. I'm doing this to make you the ruler. It's the one and only way there is."

"—"

A smile rose to Subaru's face, while Emilia's eyes flew open, shaken to the core. There was no possible way anything he said could make sense to her. And since he had told her at the outset it was raving, he did not mind if it failed to reach her. It came to Subaru's deepest wish, provided only that it bore fruit.

"Flames of ruin drive the nation to its end — and the one who cuts down the culprit behind them will be no Reinhardt, no Dragon, but you. Not one candidate left in the Selection can put up anything to outshine it. The hero who broke a four-hundred-year deadlock and saved the world is you!"

"That — that isn't — what on earth are you saying? Stop it — I don't understand! I don't understand a word of what you're saying!"

Emilia clutched her head and stopped her ears, trying to shut Subaru's words out. Tears welled in her eyes, and as he watched one spill down her pale cheek, a sweet shock struck him in the chest. Part of it was guilt at making her cry. Part of it was the sight of his own doing having shaken her heart — and the dark gladness that came of it.

"You don't have to understand. It's fine if you don't. Everything after this settles itself, once others lift you up of their own accord. Then all you have to do is have your wish granted. That is the only reason I set the country on fire. Every bit of it was for you."

"Liar — liar, liar! I mean — I — you, why would you—"

Emilia wailed at a devotion she had never looked for, at offerings she had never wanted. Of course she was grieved. Of course she could not understand. Subaru knew that he was in the wrong. Subaru knew that he was mistaken. Subaru knew that these methods of his would bring Emilia no joy. But it was the only method he had. Grant Emilia's wish, make Emilia the ruler, and show Emilia what he felt for her. With these things in mind, and knowing from the very first that he was wrong, Subaru had come here. And so Natsuki Subaru laughed. Snickered. Smirked.

"—Watch me, Emilia. See me, loathe me, remember me."

"Who — who are you? Who in the world are you…?"

Emilia asked it, her voice trembling, as Subaru came slowly toward her with his arms spread wide. Subaru closed his eyes. It felt as though he had been yearning for this forever. Yearning to answer that question, in Emilia's presence, forever —

"—My name is Natsuki Subaru."

"Su… baru…"

Only to hear that faint calling of his name was enough to send a flood of feeling surging through him. Feeling so great that, by that alone, Subaru was wholly content to have come here. With that in his heart, and praying he would get to the end of the sentence without his voice breaking under it —

"—Sin Archbishop of the Witch Cult, representing *Pride* — Natsuki Subaru!"

"A Sin Archbishop—!"

Giving the grandest introduction he could, Subaru drove all his strength into his legs and kicked off from the ground. Natsuki Subaru gathered every last scattered scrap of his strength into a single point of his body for the final sprint of his life. He had sacrificed a great many people, ill-used those who might have been his accomplices, taken his comfort in those with whom he might have shared a bond — and now, at long last, come to this darling girl.

"I'm the man who burned the world, shook the country, killed the hero — and—"

"—"

"—whom you will kill."

Feeling the impact spear through his chest, Subaru smiled. He dropped to his knees, unable to hold himself up, and pitched down, tumbling; he never reached Emilia, his body sprawling gracelessly across the stone.

"Why?"

Subaru shut his eyes, ready to take the end — when something made him lift his lids. Emilia was standing right at his side, looking down at him. One of the tears streaming from her eyes fell to his cheek, and made him open them.

"Why?"

The same question, over and over, laid one upon another. What on earth could that "why" mean, Subaru wondered. Why did you do this? Why did it have to be this way? Why did you come here to die by my hand? There were, without doubt, a great many whys folded into that single word. And he would have liked to answer all of them — but Subaru had only the barest scrap of time left. And so he let his last breath carry his answer.

"—I love you."

***

Death drew near. Old, familiar death. Every time Subaru met death, he was carried off to a dark, lonely, unknown place — and it was a lonely place indeed, truly, a place no one could bear to be alone in. Every time Subaru met death, he was sent back out of it: out into that world of blood and anguish, of tears and grief, and of the very faintest presence of love. But it was all right now. He was satisfied.

"—"

Subaru had the sense that someone, in this dark world, had whispered something to him. It was comfort, it was encouragement, it was affirmation, it was confession. But why? This was not the lover Subaru had sought. His lover was somewhere else — somewhere he was not — her dream coming true, her efforts finding their reward. Subaru had sacrificed many people, and in the end himself, for that. And so it was all right. He had no need to be saved.
