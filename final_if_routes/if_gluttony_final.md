"—Hey. Do you know my name?"

The little girl, Amue Sears, held her breath at the question.

It was an ordinary enough thing to ask, with no real shape to it — a dull question that ought to have taken neither time nor preparation to answer. If you know it, you know it. If you don't, you don't. That was all.

"—"

And yet no voice would come out of Amue, however plainly the thing was put to her.

Life was a series of choices. This much even Amue, who had only just turned fourteen, knew well enough from the little living she had done. In life, decisions must be made about very nearly everything — beginning with the small, ordinary things, running up through the large ones that turn a life one way or another. But large or small, a life was made, from end to end, of decisions. And now a question that carried more weight than any in her fourteen years had been set before Amue Sears — or rather, the greatest decision of her life. That dull, trifling, unremarkable question, asked a moment ago, was exactly that.

"—Hey. Do you know my name?"

The question, asked again, drew the cords of Amue's throat tighter. And yet the one who asked it did not seem to have meant to. To ask a second time was itself a kind of courtesy; there was something almost off-balance in the consideration of it. If it was the questioner who tormented her, it was the questioner, too, who was the more afraid. In truth she understood that he was only seeking his answer. Which was why Amue would have to grope among the choices for the right one, with no hint to go on but what she could turn over inside her own head.

*What, in the end, would be the right answer?*

To know it, or not to know it — which was the one the questioner wanted to hear? Or would it be better to say she knew it, even if she didn't? Or to say she didn't, even if she did? — Amue's heart screamed under the two agonizing options.

"—Hey. Do. You. Know. My. Name?"

Each piece of the question, asked once more, came stamped with impatience. If the two of them could not reach each other, did that make him anxious? The fear of it plucked at her chest. Honestly, she could believe that unless she answered with a plain yes or a plain no, it would be flatly impossible to meet what the questioner wanted. She could not end it still saying nothing, still giving no reply, still making no decision. She would not be let go. Of that, and that alone, there was no doubt.

"—"

Still her voice would not come. She met the black eyes that stood right before her own and turned them over in her mind. Deep in those vacant black eyes she could make out the wretched figure of Amue herself — she did not want to say it aloud, but she was conscious of how she must look, worn down to a shadow of the girl she had been. Terrified through and through by the sheer force of the one in front of her, she shrank into herself. Her face was drawn and spent, as though she had aged whole decades in a moment. If this went on she would simply die of the pressure of the question itself; fragile as she was —

"—Hey. Do you know my name?"

The heavy pressure that had felt, an instant before, like death itself now, on the contrary, kindled something like hope in her. Even as the suffocation crushed inward on her chest, a premonition wrapped itself around her: that she would answer, and then be let go. And the moment she thought it, the bleak weight that had ruled her whole body began, she realized, to lift. The suffocation had come from the fierce pressure of her own doubt over what to answer. To be freed of it, she need only bite down her fear at the first question. Some instinct of self-preservation, perhaps — but to Amue, in that moment, it came like a revelation from on high. And so, her lips trembling, she looked once more into those black eyes.

"—Hey. Do you know my name?"

She would be freed of this suffocation by answering. With all the will she had, Amue at last moved her shaking tongue and shaped the words.

"N… no…"

True to her heart, Amue said that she did not know. She loosed her thoughts from the choices that had, until now, gone spinning endlessly round her mind. She answered with perfect clearness. And in fact she knew neither the name nor the face of the one who stood before her. For a girl who lived in what one might call the borderlands — the far, remote countryside — the doings of the Kingdom, even grave ones, were like rumors out of some distant land. So however great a man he might be, to her he was a stranger she did not know —

"—Is that so."

It was a short reply. Whether some deep feeling had gone into it, or only dejection, she could not tell. But Amue's decision was made. What came after was the questioner's own affair. If a life was a series of choices and decisions, then that held true for anyone. Amue had chosen; now the questioner would have to choose as well.

"—"

The fourteen-year-old girl waited, in silence, for the questioner's choice — waited for his answer all alone, in the middle of a village where no one had been left but her.

"Hey, Subaru. Isn't it about time for lunch?"

Stopped in his tracks by the call, Natsuki Subaru rubbed his stomach. It was already past noon, and now that it was mentioned, he was indeed hungry. The reason he hadn't noticed till now was that he'd been giving the whole of himself over to the walking. The road ran on and on without end, enough that a man had to bend his entire mind to putting one foot ahead of the other; and to dwell on where it led was to sink into gloom, so absurdly long was the journey.

"Still — a break matters too, doesn't it? Sorry, Emilia-tan. You tired?"

"Not at all — I'm perfectly fine. It's only that it was on my mind, worrying over you, walking all this while. If you're all right, then it's all right, but…"

"No, no — you're right, my stomach's been rumbling too. I'm starving, near enough to have my belly stick to my backbone. Ha — how dire, how dire."

"Is that so? That would be a reaaaally dire place for them to end up, wouldn't it…"

Hearing Subaru's answer as he laid his hands over his stomach with great show, the silver-haired girl — Emilia — pressed a hand to her mouth and giggled. Her features were the prettiest in all the world, and her voice was like the ringing of a silver bell. As that smiling face drew near, Subaru scratched his head and let his own mouth curve into a smile. And, from beside the two of them —

"Don't tease Emilia too much, I suppose. However rumbly your stomach, it's not possible for your belly and your backbone to stick together, in fact."

"Whoops."

He turned toward the voice and found Beatrice there, arms folded, a composed look settled on her face. Subaru narrowed his eyes at the observation the little girl in her pretty dress had made, and Beatrice, seeing it, drew her well-kept brows together. "What, I suppose?"

"Subaru's face is a bit unsettling, in fact. What is the matter, I suppose?"

"'Rumbly' was so cute when you said it. Say it again!"

"Grrr, in fact! Don't fasten onto such strange things, I suppose!"

Face flushed, Beatrice snapped back at Subaru's foolishness. He and Emilia traded a glance and laughed at the way she'd reacted — her cheeks puffing out all the further. Seeing her like that was truly adorable. There was no mistaking it. Say it to her straight, though, and he'd only sour her mood, so, having weighed that, Subaru rolled up a sleeve with an "Right, then!"

"Well — how about we take Emilia-tan's concern to heart and get lunch started? Let me show off my skills, for the moment. Any requests?"

"Ah — in that case, I'd like you to use mayonnaise. Look, I imagine Ram's gone and made some more of it again, hasn't she?"

"Of course, of course — it's my pleeeasure, if that's Emilia-tan's order. Lucky for us there's plenty, thanks to Ram's stockpiling. She's come round at last to the charms of mayonnaise—"

"—Quit spouting such idiocy."

"Uwaaa—!"

Setting down the packs he'd been carrying, Subaru had begun making a little camp just off the road when he caught sight of a pair of slender legs planted beside him and a set of pale-pink eyes looking down on his startled face. She stood with her own arms folded, an air of stateliness about her, and not the smallest trace of warmth in her look — a stark thing against the maid's uniform she wore.

"That's a rather blunt hello, Ram. At least let a man finish his sentence."

"Ha. Don't make me laugh. I only help make the mayonnaise because it's a paid order. As if Ram would put her faith in a seasoning that's nothing but sour and white. Revolting."

"Calling it revolting is going too far! For a start, you can't deny the sheer firepower of mayonnaise on steamed potatoes! I won't have you saying that flavor's a lie!"

Mayonnaise, laid on thick over steamed potatoes still hot and salted from the pot. The sweetness of that forbidden pairing, overrunning the tongue, was an Eden every lover of mayonnaise owed it to himself to taste at least once — potatoes and mayonnaise held, past all dispute, the highest compatibility of any two things, a luster that would never dull. Subaru's mouth watered as he recalled that splendid harmony. Were the ones beside him picturing the same taste? He could see the shine come up in Emilia's and Beatrice's eyes.

"Look at that — two adorable little gourmands, and their faces say it all! Seeing that, can you still slander mayonnaise with your heresy?"

"…Fine. It's as Barusu says. The magnificence of the steamed potato is such that even a strange seasoning like mayonnaise can add something to it."

"That's not it — it's the other way round! No — even 'the other way round' isn't quite it. You're just being stubborn. Isn't that so, nee-sama!"

In the end Ram would never once grant that mayonnaise contributed more than the potato. Subaru shook a fist at the obstinacy of it, and Emilia broke into a strained little laugh, telling the pair of them, "Now, now, settle down."

"Subaru, calm down. I like mayonnaise, and I like steamed potatoes, and I like mayonnaise on steamed potatoes too. Besides — it's really just that Ram can't bring herself to be honest."

"…Emilia-sama, please stop taking it upon yourself to speak Ram's thoughts as you please. My apparent age will only drop the more you talk like that."

"S-sorry. But — what do you mean, apparent age…?"

Emilia was thrown by Ram's unreasonable, unreadable reply. And yet the feeling that lived under their exchange was nothing sullen; it was, on the contrary, an unhidable depth of affection and trust. Emilia and Ram had built between them something close to an ideal bond. To call it master and servant was a delicate matter, since Ram did not think of herself as anyone's subordinate — there was more than that between them. And only Subaru understood it well. In any case —

"I feel for you, nee-sama, but Emilia-tan's word is gospel, and it comes first. That's the way this party runs. Anyhow — today's lunch, with a whole heap of mayonnaise!"

"Yaaay — hooray!"

Emilia clapped in delight, and Ram let out a long-suffering sigh. Even so, that was as far as she'd press it. Emilia understood the taste of the thing for herself. And Subaru, being an unrepentant mayo-lover through and through, was of course the biased judge of this menu.

"And Beako loves mayonnaise every bit as much as I do. Makes you happy, doesn't it?"

"I can't deny it, in fact. But the all-knowing air you say it in is irritating, I suppose. Betty's temper isn't so easily soothed, in fact. I'll want a delicious meal for that, I suppose."

"Heh. As you wish."

Puffing out her slender chest, Beatrice made her adorable little demand, and Subaru bowed to it. After that show of ceremony — and once he'd made sure Emilia and the others were content — they set about the meal. Even so, one could hardly manage anything elaborate out in the open. With magic, of course, fire and water came easily enough, but —

"I'm sorry, Subaru. I can't really be much help."

"No need for sorry. Now's my moment to shine, isn't it? If anything — it's all right for you to fall for me all over again when I show off some skills you didn't expect."

"Honestly, you're such a fool."

Returning the smile of an Emilia who still wore a faint one of her own, Subaru set to working out how to make the meal from the ingredients on hand. Food was what fed their strength day by day; he had no wish to be careless with it. Checking through the packs, Subaru did everything he could to put together a meal that would meet every one of their needs. Everyone who traveled at his side would be given something good to eat. He hadn't thought about it before, but it would please him for everyone to have the good cooking he'd made. That, too, was fuel to him as he worked. If he could do so simple a thing this well, it ought to have delighted his mother a great deal.

"Mm-mm-mm-mm…"

Keeping that feeling penned in a corner of his mind, Subaru glanced round at those busying themselves with the cooking, and his cheeks eased at the cheerful sight of Emilia and the rest. He could even hear a little humming. It was off-key.

"Ohoo — the one on kitchen duty today is onii-sa~n. That's a bit worryi~ng."

Meili had turned up while the preparations were underway. She faced Subaru, giving him a look ill-suited to her years as she wound her fingers through her deep-blue braid, and he met it with a suspicious eye of his own at that sweetly smiling figure.

"Worrying, huh. What's worrying about it. I'll not let a girl who says such cheeky things eat!"

"Wow — that's abu~se. Besides, isn't it onii-san who tries to drown everything in may~onnaise? I don't like it much at all."

"Wh — what! You too — you don't know the charm of mayonnaise…?"

"Don't make a face like the world's about to e~nd."

Meili sighed at the astonishment on Subaru's face. But the blow her answer had dealt him was beyond measuring. Even if it fell short of the world's ending, the shock was of a size to make him feel the year, at least, had reached its end.

"Even so, I think you take it a touch too seriously. Miss Meili only meant she isn't on the same wavelength as a man who works mayonnaise into every last ingredient."

"Ugh…"

It was Julius who took up Meili's side as she aired her objection. A handsome man, grace in his very gait, he came over and, all at once, grinned at Subaru.

"If I may add my own two words — I share Miss Meili's view. Mayonnaise isn't bad in itself; it's only that its first impression fades once you put it on everything. It wants handling with some care, you know."

"You prickly, mouthy man. What was going on just now was between me and Meili. It would've been nice if you'd kept out of it."

"In my role as one of this company's members, I'd say it falls to me to preserve the health of the group. Leave the seed of a quarrel to itself and it buds; let it blossom and you'll rue and lament it far too late. It's my duty to give a word of counsel before it comes to that. And this is the reaction I get?"

"You're a man with a comeback for every single thing I say, aren't you!"

Expressing himself in that roundabout way, Julius pricked at each of Subaru's little indiscretions, and Subaru, feeling cornered by the words, let his voice roughen. And Emilia, having heard the exchange, broke into a laugh — a soft "Heh."

"Emilia-tan?"

"As ever, Julius and Subaru are reaaally good friends."

Subaru's shoulders slumped in dejection under Emilia's gaze, which looked on the pair as though at something delightful. Standing in the same boat, Julius closed one eye and ran a hand back through his fringe. For once, it seemed, his opinion and Subaru's had met — though the way Emilia received it was another matter entirely, her jewel-like amethyst eyes taking the exchange between the two of them for the byplay of close friends.

"Um — Emilia-tan. I'm always saying this, but that's a—"

"That's a?"

It was always a great misunderstanding, whatever the circumstances. Subaru went to explain as much to Emilia, who had tipped her head in puzzlement —

"That's a…"

"—"

The words he'd meant to go on with caught at their very start, and his lip stiffened. Just then, Emilia's motion — head tilted, smiling before his eyes — came to a standstill. No — it was not Emilia alone who stopped. All around him, everyone who a moment ago had been busy with the cooking, everyone mid-sentence, halted where they stood. Every one of them went utterly still — as though the world's time had come to a stop, waiting on Subaru's next move. But the sound of the wind, the smell of the green, the boiling water all said otherwise. The world had not stopped. Only Emilia and the others had — as if Subaru stood waiting for the picture to catch up to him, trying to call them back into being. But it was only Emilia and the rest who had stopped —

"—Master! Ta-daa — I went and scouted on ahead!"

"—"

All at once an outside presence forced its way into the silent, stopped world. It came at Subaru where he crouched feeding the fire atop a boulder, springing nimbly, diving at him with tremendous vigor.

"Behold the swift return of Shaula! Master, praise me and hug me and love me!"

A tall, beautiful woman, a smile on her face, turned to Subaru and said it — a woman who left her clear white skin bare and wore her long brown hair tied up, flaunting her frankly sensual figure without a shred of shame. Shaula, letting an easy smile drift across her face.

"—"

Subaru's response to Shaula's arrival, though, was a poor one. And that was only to be expected. She had come barging in at the very moment Emilia was smiling. Her wildly lively leap had scattered Emilia and, with her, wiped away all the others who had stood there still. Subaru frowned at the fact of it.

"Wh — what? What's the matter, Master? Did I go and mess up again?"

"…No. It's fine. Don't worry about it. You didn't do anything wrong."

"Is that so? Then I won't worry! Ah — besides that, Master, I'm glad you're the one making the meal! I'm staaarving—"

For a moment Shaula's face had clouded at the look on his, but the instant she heard him take it back she went to fold him in an embrace, not a trace of her earlier worry left on her. And even with Shaula's body pressing against his arm, that hellish softness against his elbow and shoulder, Subaru shook her off with a blunt, "Hey — off."

"Ah! Master's so cruel… But I won't be put off. Bang, bang, bang — attacking this way, I'll snipe clean through Master's heart, his soul, and his manly instincts."

"You're aiming for the dead center, is what that is… And don't go grabbing the arms of a man in the middle of cooking. What'll you do if I burn my hand?"

"If that happened, I'd gently lick the hurt part! Lick it and lick it, day and night, and never let go!"

"That wouldn't do a thing to bring the swelling down," Subaru said, flat.

Shaula pressed her case, both arms thrown up with vigor, and Subaru shrugged at the attitude and gave a feeble laugh — at which she raised her brows with a "So — so!"

"Did Master just laugh? Did something funny happen?"

"What's funny is you. Just you."

"Ehh — is that… a proposal…?"

"How on earth do you get there!"

He'd no idea how the talk had leapt so far, but Subaru poked Shaula in the forehead — her cheeks working, dyed red — putting into it all his denial and objection, and Shaula groaned an "Ow—"

"Come now — doesn't saying 'you're a funny woman' amount to a man and a woman's lawful right to fall for each other? After a line like that, oughtn't you to go straight on with 'be my woman'?"

"Honestly — where do you pick up such one-sided information? Even where I come from that pattern's gone stale; you hardly see it anymore…"

Maybe it wasn't so rare, the way a male lead would handle a first meeting like this in games made for women, or in shoujo manga. But whether the thing was still in fashion, Subaru couldn't have said. At any rate, he had not called Shaula funny in order to charm her. And yet, as far as Subaru was concerned, Shaula's reaction was —

"—What you said was unexpected. It was new."

"—"

"That's why talking with you isn't… so bad. I've no doubt of that at all."

He wouldn't go so far as to say that talking with anyone but Shaula was painful. But when Subaru spoke with other people, it would, naturally, so often be brought home to him how unfinished he was. And that was painful. It wasn't that anyone was at fault. The one at fault was, always, himself.

"The one at fault… is always me. Which is why…"

"So in other words — this time it's a proposal!"

"What the hell — no!"

The mood of that earnest little speech lasted all of a second. Shaula rained her discontent down on the shouting Subaru, wiggling her hands and feet with an "Ehh—"

"But, but — in that place where I passed my days in idle grey, it was you who blew a new wind into me. It's like that. Doesn't that make it a lawful right within a lawful right?"

"Absolutely not. So — where in the world does that skewed store of knowledge of yours come from…?"

He'd meant to have a fairly serious talk, but Shaula had broken it clean apart, and so Subaru gave the idea up. Cheeks slackening despite himself, he went back to bending his attention on the cooking. There beside Subaru, cooking by the roadside, a meal was laid for the two of them. For the two — Subaru and Shaula — and not for those who ought to have been there enjoying the talk: not for Emilia, Beatrice, Ram, Meili, Julius, nor any of the others. Realizing that truth once again, Subaru's chest ached with a cruel and desolate grief.

Time and again he was made to feel that he was an unfinished thing. And it frayed him raw. That it was no healthy state of mind was plain past any argument — but drop a man into the situation itself, and he would come to know the feel of a soul worn through. And there was no doubt, frankly, that this was the very state Natsuki Subaru's mind was in now.

"—Master, Master, where are we bound for next?"

"Ah — right…"

Shaula walked at Subaru's side, humming, in high spirits. He looked up at her question and turned it over as he gazed off up the road ahead.

"For now I'd say we head west, wouldn't you? Are you perhaps in search of civilization? If it's away from civilization you want, then bear right, and you may plunge straight over the Great Waterfall — one can hope."

"Don't put such a frightening notion forward, nee-sama… That's the only feeling I'll be left with, you know."

It was not plain advice; it was said in the way that was Ram's own, the manner of one trading a little poison across the table. Keeping that close in his chest, Subaru resolved to head west as she'd counseled.

"I'm going to keep on west… But — what will you do?"

"Eh? Why, I'll follow after Master and never once let go. The time we spent parted was so, so long that it's grown hard for me to be away from Master even a second. Do take good care of your Shaula, who watches over you from good morning clear round to good morning again."

"From good morning to good morning, you say… How frightening! That'd be the whole day round!"

"And all the night besides."

Shuddering at that grandly stalkerish notice of intent, Subaru dropped his face, a troubled look settling on it. In all honesty it felt strange, that he should have ended up walking beside Shaula. There was the Subaru who carried a shameful weight of guilt and wretchedness. And there was Shaula, who acted openly, without a single inhibition. Perhaps, as a pair, the tension between plus and minus was held at some equilibrium. But in any case — much as he certainly felt Shaula's manner was a help to him, he could not think what merit there could be, from her side, in keeping him company.

"Master is Master. That's the number-one thing, for me."

"…Honestly, I don't get you at all. Do you really mean to keep trailing after me?"

"Of courrse! Haven't you heard the phrase 'till death do us part'! I can't let the love that banked up over all the time we were apart run dry, you know. I won't leave Master's side again, whatever happens. Why, I'll even bear you children — enough to field a whole baseball team!"

"Don't go hurling such horrifying fastballs around…"

Thrusting out her ample chest, Shaula looked at him with shining eyes, and he covered his own with his palm. What lay at the floor of his heart was a tangled, unfathomable thing. He couldn't have said whether Shaula's rather blunt conduct amounted to flirting; but in itself, as a man, Subaru was not exactly displeased by it. Set her oddness aside, and Shaula was a beautiful woman, and no small draw.

"—"

And yet Natsuki Subaru's heart could not be moved by Shaula's coaxing. For feeling as human as that was a thing permitted only to proper human beings. Which is to say — it was, for the man he was now, a faculty flatly out of reach.

"I think Subaru might do to be a little kinder to Shaula. She keeps giving it everything she has, and even so — isn't it a little sad?"

Of a sudden a voice like the chime of a silver bell cut into his hesitation. Turning his eyes to his side, he found Emilia peering into his face, and he closed one eye at that adorable, pouting look.

"…For all that it's Emilia-tan saying it — don't you think that was rather harsh? And here I'd thought I understood how I felt about you."

"Um… I suppose. Maybe. Sorry. Of course I'm lonely too. But I think it does no good to keep moping about it forever. After all…"

"After all?"

"After all — I'm already—"

Narrowing her amethyst eyes, Emilia went to carry on the words. He'd have liked to cut in at once, if there were something he didn't want to hear — but that was hard. To close his ears to this Emilia's words was harder than closing them to the word of God itself. And yet —

"Whoops."

Even if he couldn't bring himself to interrupt her, an outside thing might do it for him. Catching a noise drawing up from behind, Subaru turned his attention from Emilia and stepped to the roadside; the sound of wheels told him a dragon carriage was coming on. In one motion he took Shaula by the arm and the two of them cleared the carriage's path.

"Yheh — Master's forward today…"

"Off."

Subaru roughly fended off the embrace Shaula tried to make of the pulled arm, and, shaken loose, she went crashing into the trees along the road with a shriek — "GYAAA." Sparing a glance back at Shaula, nose gone red, still colliding with the trunks, he waited for the dragon carriage to pass out of sight and stepped back onto the road —

"Hm?"

Up ahead, the carriage that had gone by the two of them let out a sound and drew to a halt. It did not seem a trouble with the carriage itself. Which left him no reason for the stop but Subaru and Shaula. It was still the height of the day, yet the road was deserted, and for a split second Subaru's guard went up, as though he'd met with bandits.

"—You wouldn't happen to be Natsuki Subaru-san, by any chance?"

But that guard fell away just as fast at the call of the one who now climbed down from the driver's seat. Having spoken his name, the tall youth who ought to have been at the reins came walking over to the Subaru who had let his wariness go. He was near Subaru's own age, grey hair drawn back in a ponytail; his mild features left no strong impression, but he was, unexpectedly, solidly built — not a man who looked to do heavy labor, though his work seemed to involve at least some of it. First impressions aside, what mattered most to Subaru was that this youth knew him, and had called out his name.

"Ah — it's as I thought, it is Natsuki-san, isn't it? It's been a long time."

"Ah — well…"

The youth greeted him with the ease of an acquaintance beyond doubt, for all that there'd been some distance between them. Hesitating over how to answer, Subaru cast a look about him. Catching it, Emilia, Beatrice, and the rest shook their heads — signaling that they'd no notion who the man was, either. Emilia and the others, and Subaru with them, had thrown up their hands; and still he balked at striking up a conversation while feigning acquaintance. That was Subaru's stock-in-trade, but for a number of reasons he didn't want to reach for it now. Seeing Subaru mired in confusion in the state he was in, the youth drew his shapely brows together and said,

"Oh — perhaps you've forgotten? It's me — Regin Suwen. My elder brother, Otto Suwen, is a help to you. We met once before, at the village."

"Regin, you say… Ah — that Otto's!"

"When you put it that way — 'that Otto' — you make it sound as though he were no concern of yours, no?"

Managing a strained smile at the unnatural phrasing, Subaru clapped his hands in comprehension before the youth — Regin. He could not, unfortunately, place the man's figure; but Otto he knew. Now that it was said, Regin and Otto did share a great deal in their features. There was no doubting they were brothers — and acquaintances of Subaru's, besides.

"My fault, my fault. It's just you've caught me at a moment when all manner of things are churning in my head. That's why your name didn't come to me straight off — something like that."

"No, no — I imagine it can't be helped, in a busy position like yours. The Royal Selection has reached even my ears, out here in the back country… My brother isn't causing you any trouble, is he?"

"Otto-kun, causing trouble… what a preposterous thing to say. Every one of us has been helped in all sorts of ways, thanks to Otto-kun."

"Yeah. Otto's become a real help to all of us in the camp… something like that. If that guy weren't around, it seems like things'd be a good deal more scattered."

"Is that… so. Then that's good…"

Regin voiced a vague sort of gratitude at that, and seemed genuinely relieved to be told how his elder brother was faring. After that his eyes flitted between Subaru and the Shaula standing a little apart from him.

"Even so, Natsuki-san — what are you doing in a place like this? It must be hard going, walking the high road without so much as a dragon carriage to ride…"

*—"Murder becomes a habit."*

That was one of the phrases the famous detective Hercule Poirot left to the world. The sense of it was not a man who, having killed once, wakes of a sudden with a taste for slaughter and repeats the crime to feed the craving. It meant, rather, one who has once solved his trouble by murder — for whom, thereafter, whenever a fresh trouble arises, the thought comes to break through it by murder again.

*—"Murder becomes a habit."*

But if killing truly was bound up with the solving of the problem, could it even be called the fruit of murder turned to habit? Might it not be said that murder had not become a habit at all, but that unavoidable circumstances had simply sprung up? The case was not that one added murder to one's options because it had grown habitual, but that there was no option other than to kill. To speak of murder becoming a habit was, perhaps, no longer possible.

*—"Murder becomes."*

To hell with Hercule Poirot. A detective out of a story needn't put on such airs. How could he speak as though he saw clean through it all? The reasons behind every situation, behind everything, are snarled together in a hundred complications, layer upon layer of them. Don't sweep those aside and make light of it, forcing the one phrase over the whole of the world.

*—"Murder."*

This was the only road when there was no other. It was the most reasonable, when there was no other way to the right answer. Which was why it was not that murder becomes a habit, but rather —

*—"Only murder becomes the answer."*

Yes. That was it. Of course it was. That alone was the answer. That alone was the right answer. That alone would cut through the hopelessness of this dead end; it was the last resort.

Patching together, patching together, going on filling in its colors.

Patching together, patching together, patching together, going on bringing himself nearer to whole.

Patching together, patching together, patching together — there is too much to be patched together. Even if he patched it on until the original shape was past knowing, still he could patch together a new one.

Patching together, and going on living.

Patching together, and going on living.

Until the day should come that he died, he would go on patching it together, and living on —
